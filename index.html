Abstract

Biological knowledge graphs integrate heterogeneous data across molecular, cellular, and phenotypic domains, but graph traversal treats mechanistic relationships, statistical associations, and annotation edges equivalently, propagating signal along biologically invalid paths and making causal and non-causal explanations computationally indistinguishable. Standard enrichment approaches compound this problem: they lose statistical power at small gene set sizes and collapse numerically at the large gene set sizes produced by rare variant aggregation.

We introduce the Biological Information Flow Ontology (BIFO), a formal specification of admissible biological entities, states, and the directional information flows between them. Applied to a heterogeneous knowledge graph, BIFO conditioning produces a constrained propagation substrate in which signal traverses only biologically valid paths. We implement this as BIFO-PPR, combining BIFO conditioning with Personalized PageRank propagation and degree-normalized pathway scoring. BIFO-PPR is the implementation through which we evaluate BIFO; the experiments that follow test whether ontology-grounded constraints on information flow produce measurable improvements in pathway inference relative to standard methods that treat all edges equivalently. In a three-arm ablation, we establish that pathway inference in ontology-aligned knowledge graphs depends on a structurally distinct bridge layer: mechanistic-only propagation yields zero pathway scores because pathway nodes are unreachable through mechanistic edges alone, while BIFO-conditioned propagation with explicit bridge admission enables signal transfer between the molecular and pathway annotation layers.

Across a curated congenital heart disease benchmark, BIFO-PPR achieves P@10 = 0.70 versus 0.30 for correctly implemented Fisher enrichment and 0.10 for propagation-based baselines. In two independent rare variant cohorts from the Kids First program, BIFO-PPR recovers a coherent ciliopathy pathway cluster in both congenital heart disease and neuroblastoma. WP_CILIOPATHIES carries the strongest statistical enrichment signal under the rewiring null among well-calibrated pathways in KF-CHD (null_z = 41.2, q = 0.008, rank 43 of 2,130) and ranks third in KF-NBL (null_z = 18.4, q = 0.014). Correctly implemented Fisher enrichment independently ranks WP_CILIOPATHIES first in both cohorts, supporting that the ciliopathy signal reflects underlying biology. BIFO-PPR additionally recovers this signal where Fisher enrichment loses discriminative power or pathway-level specificity.

BIFO provides a principled approach to a problem that affects any inference task on heterogeneous biological graphs: the inability to distinguish biologically meaningful signal from incidental connectivity when edge types are treated equivalently.

Introduction

Modern biology generates large-scale, multi-modal datasets spanning genomic variation, gene expression, chromatin architecture, protein interactions, metabolism, and disease phenotypes. Knowledge graphs have emerged as a leading framework for integrating these data types, representing biological entities and their relationships in a structure that supports cross-domain queries and biological reasoning [1,2,3]. However, integration alone does not enable inference. One of the central challenges in using these graphs is understanding how interpretable biological signal can flow across a network from one domain of information to another: from gene-level molecular events to pathway-level biological programs, from genetic variants to disease phenotypes, or from drug targets to downstream cellular effects.

Biological function arises from directional, causal processes that are well established by decades of experimental observation: transcription proceeds from gene to RNA, signaling cascades propagate from upstream activators to downstream effectors, and metabolic transformations are constrained by biochemical feasibility and spatial context. These processes define not only which entities are connected, but which transformations between them are biologically meaningful. Individual entity classes in biomedical graphs are reasonably well specified by dedicated vocabularies: genes by HGNC [4], transcripts by GENCODE [5], proteins by UniProtKB [6], and phenotypes by HPO [7]. What has not existed is a unified definitional layer that ties these entity definitions together into a formal specification of admissible information flow between them: which transitions between entity classes are biologically valid, in which direction they operate, and what it means for signal to propagate from one domain to another. In heterogeneous knowledge graphs, this gap means that mechanistic relationships, statistical associations, and annotation edges are encoded within the same structure, and graph traversal treats them equivalently, collapsing causal and non-causal connections into a single topology. A path connecting a genetic variant to a disease phenotype may reflect a valid mechanistic process, an indirect regulatory association, or an incidental correlation; without a formal definition of admissibility, these possibilities are computationally indistinguishable.

Standard approaches to pathway enrichment analysis perform reliably on focused, biologically coherent gene sets, typically in the range of tens to a few hundred genes, but encounter hard statistical limits outside that range [8]. For small gene sets, overlap statistics are unstable and dominated by incidental matches. For large gene sets, particularly those derived from rare variant aggregation across disease cohorts, p-values collapse to numerical limits and lose discriminative power regardless of implementation. Graph-based neighborhood expansion introduces a complementary problem. Biological knowledge graphs exhibit small-world and scale-free topology [9,10], meaning that any seed set reaches a large fraction of the full graph within just a few hops. In the DDKG, median shortest path lengths between gene concept nodes are on the order of three hops [2], which is why neighborhood-based enrichment on such graphs effectively queries the entire graph and loses all discriminating power. These are not failures of any particular tool but reflect the statistical and topological properties of the problem: overlap-based enrichment works best when the input gene set is small and coherent, and neighborhood expansion works best when the graph has meaningful local structure rather than near-universal connectivity.

Existing frameworks address parts of this problem but do not resolve it. Ontologies such as Gene Ontology and HPO provide structured representations of biological concepts but do not define propagation rules. Knowledge graph systems support typed traversal but rely on user-defined queries rather than biologically grounded constraints. Causal frameworks such as BEL [11] and GO-CAM [12] encode mechanistic relationships but operate within curated representations and do not provide a general protocol for conditioning arbitrary heterogeneous graphs. Graph machine learning approaches can operate over heterogeneous structures but learn propagation behavior from data rather than enforcing biologically interpretable constraints.

To address this gap, we introduce the Biological Information Flow Ontology (BIFO), an ontological framework that formally defines admissible biological entities, entity states, and the information flows between them. BIFO specifies what kinds of biological entities can exist in a flow-aware network, what transformations between those entities are biologically meaningful, and in which directions information can propagate. Within this framework, we distinguish three categories of relationships. Mechanistic relationships encode directional, state-changing biological transformations, such as signal transduction, transcriptional regulation, and biochemical catalysis, and participate in propagation. Observational relationships, such as statistical co-expression or text-mining co-occurrence, capture correlative associations without encoding causal state transitions and are excluded from propagation. Contextual relationships encode spatial, temporal, or structural constraints that restrict which transitions are feasible without themselves carrying signal. Applied to a heterogeneous knowledge graph, these definitions produce a constrained propagation substrate: the constraint is a consequence of the definitions, not their purpose. BIFO is not designed to restrict graphs; it is designed to formalize what biology is in terms of admissible entities and flows. We refer to the full implemented system combining BIFO conditioning with Personalized PageRank propagation and degree-normalized pathway scoring as BIFO-PPR throughout this paper, to distinguish the implemented pipeline from the ontology specification.

We show in this paper that biological inference can be improved by restricting propagation to the admissible transformations defined by BIFO, rather than by adding information to a graph. By constraining propagation to semantically valid paths, BIFO-PPR reduces the reachable state space and increases the identifiability of biologically meaningful signal, inducing a directed, non-symmetric adjacency operator that encodes the source-sink asymmetry inherent in biological processes. Although we evaluate BIFO-PPR here in a gene-to-pathway inference setting using rare variant cohort data, the underlying formalism applies to any feature-to-feature inference problem on a heterogeneous biological graph where the identifiability of causal from associative relationships limits inference.

We evaluate this framework using a controlled benchmark derived from the Data Distillery Knowledge Graph (DDKG) [3], integrating curated congenital heart disease gene sets with pathway annotations. Through a three-arm ablation design, we show that mechanistic-only propagation is insufficient to recover pathway-level biological programs, as pathway nodes are structurally unreachable through mechanistic edges alone in this graph representation. This finding reflects a two-layer architecture in ontology-aligned biomedical knowledge graphs, in which mechanistic gene-gene relationships and curated pathway annotations occupy structurally distinct layers connected only through a formally defined bridge edge class. Introducing BIFO-conditioned propagation enables signal transfer across these layers, consistent with this architecture.

We further evaluate BIFO-PPR using rare variant cohorts from the Kids First program [13]. In this regime, gene sets derived from variant aggregation are large and heterogeneous, and standard enrichment approaches become numerically unstable, implementation-sensitive, or lose discriminative power. BIFO-PPR concentrates distributed signal across the graph and recovers biologically relevant pathways without prior specification. In two independent pediatric cohorts representing congenital heart disease and neuroblastoma, diseases with known epidemiological co-occurrence and shared neural crest developmental origins [14], BIFO-PPR independently recovers a coherent ciliopathy pathway cluster, with convergent support from correctly implemented Fisher enrichment.

Together, these results establish BIFO as an ontological framework for formally defining admissible biological information flow, and BIFO-PPR as an implemented system that enables mechanistically grounded pathway inference where standard approaches fail.

Methods

These Methods describe the BIFO framework and its application to benchmark evaluation and cohort analysis. All analyses follow a common pipeline: (1) BIFO conditioning defines an admissible edge set, (2) a directed propagation operator is constructed, (3) signal is propagated via Personalized PageRank, (4) pathway scores are computed at pathway concept nodes, and (5) significance is assessed using empirical null models. Parts I through III define this core pipeline: graph conditioning, propagation, and significance testing. Parts IV and V describe benchmark evaluation and cohort application. Across all analyses, pathway scores are evaluated against an empirical rewiring null that tests whether recovered signals exceed what is expected from graph topology alone. The specific analyses instantiated on this pipeline and their relationship to the paper’s results are described in Section 2.

Part I: Graph and conditioning

1 BIFO conditioning

1.1 Ontology design

BIFO transforms a heterogeneous knowledge graph into a propagation-ready structure by restricting information flow to edges consistent with biologically admissible transformations. Starting from an input graph \(G = (V, E)\), each edge is classified according to a predefined mapping that assigns it to one of four categories: mechanistic, weak-mechanistic, observational, or contextual. This mapping is implemented through a fixed configuration (bifo_mapping.yaml v0.7.1; 252 predicate-to-flow entries, 96 explicit non-flow designations, 46 observational edge definitions) and is applied uniformly across all analyses.

Propagation is restricted to edges designated as admissible, defined as the union of mechanistic edges, selected weak-mechanistic relationships, and gene-to-pathway membership relationships. Edges classified as observational or contextual are retained in the conditioning output for completeness but are excluded from the propagation operator. This produces a conditioned graph \(G_C = (V, E_C)\), where \(E_C \subseteq E\) contains only admissible edges.

Within the admissible edge set, gene-to-pathway membership relationships are treated as a distinct class, referred to as bridge edges, which connect molecular entities to pathway-level representations. These edges do not represent direct mechanistic interactions; rather, they enable signal transfer between the molecular layer of the graph and the pathway annotation layer. Without them, signal propagating through mechanistic gene-gene relationships has no path to pathway nodes, making pathway-level inference structurally impossible regardless of how strong the molecular signal is. BIFO admits bridge edges as a formally defined propagation-eligible class, with signal flowing unidirectionally from gene nodes to pathway nodes. Only gene-to-pathway membership edges are admissible for propagation; the reverse direction (pathway-to-gene) is explicitly excluded and classified as nonpropagating in the conditioning output. All other admissible edges operate within the molecular layer and represent mechanistic or near-mechanistic biological relationships.

Mechanistic edges encode directional, state-changing biological transformations such as protein-protein signaling, transcriptional regulation, or biochemical reactions. Weak-mechanistic edges represent relationships with partial or indirect mechanistic support that may contribute to information transfer in specific contexts. Observational edges capture statistical or correlative associations without encoding state transitions and are excluded from propagation. Contextual edges encode spatial, temporal, or structural constraints; they are retained in the conditioning output for entity typing purposes but do not carry signal.

Rather than modeling a specific biological system, BIFO defines a set of permissible transformation paths between entities, allowing propagation behavior to be evaluated in terms of biologically grounded information flow across heterogeneous data sources.

BIFO classifies each predicate type into one of these categories and assigns a classification tier that determines its role in the propagation operator. The classification is deliberately conservative: when a predicate type has mixed evidence quality across its instances, the weaker classification applies. This prevents well-supported mechanistic relationships from being diluted by observational instances of the same predicate type in the same graph.

1.2 Entity resolution

Entity resolution maps each concept node to its source vocabulary, which is necessary to apply source-specific flow class rules and membership constraints. In a standards-compliant graph, nodes carry CURIE-formatted identifiers (e.g., HGNC:1100, GO:0007165) from which the source authority can be read directly from the prefix. Where CURIEs are not available, source authority base (SAB) identifiers or equivalent vocabulary tags serve the same role. The pipeline uses a two-level procedure: at Level 1, it attempts to resolve each node to a source vocabulary by reading its identifier prefix or SAB annotation; at Level 2, edges with at least one unresolvable endpoint are dropped rather than admitted with unknown classification.

Resolution rate depends on the scope of the graph export and the vocabulary coverage of the SAB selection. Nodes belonging to vocabularies outside the configured SAB set are treated as unresolvable and their edges are excluded from the propagating operator. This is conservative by design: admitting edges of unknown provenance would undermine the flow class guarantees that BIFO conditioning provides.

1.3 Propagating operator construction

The conditioned edge set \(E_C\) is used to build a sparse directed adjacency matrix over the node set \(V\). Edge weights are uniform (weight = 1); the pipeline supports evidence-tier weighting when confidence annotations are present in the edge file, but all analyses reported here use unit weights. Row normalization is applied before transpose to produce the column-stochastic matrix used in the PPR recurrence.

Formal definition. Let \(G = (V, E)\) be the input property graph where each edge \(e = (u, v, p)\) carries predicate \(p\) and endpoint source vocabulary identifiers \(s_u, s_v\). BIFO conditioning defines a constraint operator \(C\) that produces the conditioned propagation graph \(G_C = (V, E_C)\), where:

\[E_C = \{ e \in E : \operatorname{flow\_class}(\text{predicate}(e)) \in F_{\text{admissible}} \land \text{source}(u) \text{ resolved} \land \text{source}(v) \text{ resolved} \}\](1)

\(F_{\text{admissible}}\) is the set of flow classes designated as propagating in bifo_mapping.yaml (mechanistic and weak-mechanistic tiers, plus Pathway Contribution bridge edges; excluding Observational Association, contextual_constraint, and nonpropagating_context tiers). The operator’s admissibility decisions depend only on the predicate-to-flow mapping and entity resolution rules, not on graph topology, so the same conditioning logic applies to any property graph whose edges carry compatible predicate vocabularies.

2 Knowledge graph source

The analyses in this paper use the Common Fund Data Ecosystem Data Distillery Knowledge Graph (DDKG), built on the Unified Biomedical Knowledge Graph (UBKG) and inheriting Petagraph infrastructure [2,3]. DDKG integrates heterogeneous biological knowledge from multiple source ontologies and databases into a unified concept-and-relationship graph. Each concept node carries a SAB identifier and each edge carries a predicate drawn from the source ontology’s relation vocabulary, making edge provenance fully traceable and satisfying the predicate-mapping and entity resolution requirements of BIFO conditioning.

For each analysis, the graph was queried as a 1-hop neighborhood centered on the relevant seed gene set, producing a mechanistic and association edge file and a separate gene-to-pathway membership edge file derived from MSigDB [15,16], WikiPathways [17], and Gene Ontology annotations. These were merged into a single edge file for conditioning. Node metadata was exported separately. The specific graph parameters, edge counts, and conditioning results for each analysis are described in the relevant benchmark and cohort sections.

The analyses described above are instantiated on a set of graph projections and gene sets constructed to isolate distinct properties of the method. All benchmarks and cohort analyses use the same underlying DDKG representation and BIFO conditioning procedure, with differences arising from the choice of seed sets, graph export scope, and evaluation regime.

The primary benchmark is defined on a controlled 1-hop projection of the DDKG centered on a curated set of congenital heart disease genes, producing a graph with fixed topology and known pathway reference structure (Section 6.3). Within this graph, separate benchmark cohorts are defined for discovery and recovery tasks, using curated disease genes and pathway-derived gene sets (Sections 6.1–6.4). This design allows evaluation of pathway inference under both indirect (discovery) and direct (recovery) signal regimes.

To evaluate robustness to input gene selection, an exhaustive resampling analysis is performed over all C(15,10) = 3,003 possible seed/held-out partitions of the CHD gene pool, holding the graph structure and propagation operators fixed while varying only the seed vector (Section 9). This isolates the contribution of seed composition to pathway-level inference independently of graph topology.

The same pipeline is then applied to two large-scale rare variant cohorts from the Kids First program (congenital heart disease and neuroblastoma), where gene sets are derived from germline variant aggregation and evaluated in discovery mode without predefined pathway targets (Sections 10–15). These cohort analyses represent a distinct regime from the curated benchmarks, characterized by large, heterogeneous gene sets and distributed biological signal, and are used to assess whether BIFO-conditioned propagation recovers coherent pathway structure under conditions where standard enrichment approaches lose discriminative power.

With the conditioned graph \(G_C\) and propagation operator defined, Parts II and III describe how signal is propagated and scored.

Part II: Propagation and pathway scoring

3 Personalized PageRank propagation

Signal propagation uses Personalized PageRank (PPR), also known as random walk with restart [18]. PPR models the probability that a random walk starting from a set of seed nodes visits each other node in the graph, with a restart probability that returns the walk to the seeds at each step. Applied to a BIFO-conditioned graph, this concentrates signal in the neighborhood of the seeds while respecting the directional constraints imposed by the admissible edge set.

Given the column-stochastic adjacency matrix \(\tilde{A}\) derived from \(G_C\), a seed vector \(\mathbf{s}\) with uniform mass \(1/|S|\) over seed nodes and zero elsewhere, and restart probability \(\alpha\), the PPR score vector \(\mathbf{f}\) satisfies:

\[\mathbf{f} = (1 - \alpha) \cdot \tilde{A}^\top \cdot \mathbf{f} + \alpha \cdot \mathbf{s}\](2)

The fixed point is computed iteratively as \(\mathbf{f}_{t+1} = (1-\alpha)\tilde{A}^\top\mathbf{f}_t + \alpha\mathbf{s}\), terminating when \(\|\mathbf{f}_{t+1} - \mathbf{f}_t\|_1 < 10^{-10}\) or after 500 iterations.

The restart probability \(\alpha\) controls the trade-off between local signal retention and global diffusion. High \(\alpha\) keeps signal concentrated near the seeds; low \(\alpha\) allows it to diffuse further into the graph. For pathway scoring, relevant pathway nodes may be several hops from the seed genes, so some diffusion is necessary. A value of \(\alpha = 0.5\) was selected to balance these considerations and was fixed before any benchmark evaluation.

4 Pathway scoring

4.1 Membership map construction

Pathway scoring requires a map from gene nodes to the pathway nodes they belong to. Membership is determined from gene-to-pathway edges using the designated bridge edge predicate types, drawn from the pre-conditioning edge file. Because gene-to-pathway Pathway Contribution edges are admissible under BIFO by definition and are not selectively excluded, using the pre-conditioning edge file is equivalent to using the conditioned edge set for membership construction. Membership edges are source-vocabulary constrained: a gene is counted as a member of a pathway only if its SAB matches the pathway’s source vocabulary, preventing cross-vocabulary membership contamination. Duplicate memberships are removed.

Pathways are filtered by size, retaining those with at least 8 and at most 300 members. Pathways below the minimum are too small for enrichment analysis to be meaningful; pathways above the maximum are too broad to be biologically informative. A name-pattern filter additionally excludes gene expression quantile sets and microRNA sets, which represent statistical partitions rather than curated biological programs. The resulting pathway universe is held constant across all scoring arms and baseline methods within a given analysis.

4.2 Pathway score computation

The primary scoring function, degree_norm, is defined as:

\[\text{score}(p) = \frac{f_{\text{direct}}(p)}{\sqrt{|\text{members}(p)|}}\](3)

where \(f_{\text{direct}}(p)\) is the PPR score on the pathway concept node itself, representing signal mass that arrived via bridge edges, and \(|\text{members}(p)|\) is the source-vocabulary-constrained member gene count. The square root penalty down-weights large generic pathways, which accumulate high PPR scores by virtue of having many members, without fully normalizing by size in a way that would over-penalize legitimate large biological programs. Pathways with no member genes receive a score of zero.

This scoring function was specified before benchmark evaluation and was not modified after the first successful run. A diagnostic scoring variant, local_bg, is computed as the direct PPR score minus the mean PPR score of the pathway node’s immediate graph neighbors. This variant is included in output files for exploratory analysis but is not used in any reported evaluation metrics.

Part III: Significance testing

5 Empirical significance via membership rewiring null

BIFO pathway scores are made actionable by comparison to an empirical null that tests whether a pathway’s observed score exceeds what is expected given the propagated signal landscape. The null model uses degree-preserving rewiring of the bridge edge layer, keeping the seed set and propagation operator fixed while randomizing which genes are assigned as members of each pathway. For each permutation, bridge edges are rewired via random endpoint swaps that preserve each pathway’s member count and each gene’s pathway membership count exactly. PPR is rerun on the rewired graph with the same seeds and degree_norm scores are recomputed for all pathways. This process is repeated N = 1,000 times to build a per-pathway null distribution.

Statistical significance is assessed using the finite-sample-corrected empirical p-value (Phipson and Smyth 2010):

\[p = \frac{1 + \text{count}(\text{null} \geq \text{observed})}{1 + N}\]

BH correction is applied across all scored pathways to obtain q-values. The null_z score for each pathway is the number of standard deviations the observed degree_norm score sits above the mean of its null distribution. Rewiring is restricted to gene-to-pathway membership (bridge) edges; all mechanistic edges remain fixed across permutations. The null seed universe is restricted to genes connected to pathways retained after size and name-pattern filtering; genes connected only to excluded pathways do not contribute to null permutations.

5.1 Null calibration

The rewiring null can become degenerate for pathways where the null distribution mean is near zero. In this case all permutations produce scores far below the observed value, the null standard deviation collapses, and null_z inflates mechanically rather than reflecting biological enrichment. Pathways where the null mean is less than 10% of the observed score are excluded from BH correction and reported with null_z = NaN and empirical_q = NaN. The null_calibrated column in the output flags which pathways pass this filter.

5.2 Dependence on graph composition

The validity of the rewiring null depends on the ratio of bridge edges to non-bridge edges in the propagating graph. When non-bridge edges provide sufficient routing structure, rewired bridge assignments that do not match the biological signal in the seeds produce low pathway scores, and the null distribution is well separated from the observed score. When bridge edges dominate the propagating graph, rewiring effectively randomizes the primary routing structure, and random bridge assignments frequently inflate the null mean. The bridge edge fraction therefore determines whether the rewiring null is a meaningful calibration tool for a given analysis, and is reported alongside null calibration results for each analysis in Parts IV and V.

5.3 Member-level empirical null

A complementary null tests whether a pathway’s constituent genes carry disproportionate propagated signal, without requiring PPR reruns. For each pathway, the observed mean PPR score across its member genes is compared against N = 1,000 matched random gene sets of the same size drawn from the pathway-connected gene universe. Null genes are matched to true pathway members on two structural features only: conditioned graph out-degree and pathway membership count, both log-binned into 10 quantile bins. Observed propagated score is intentionally excluded from matching to avoid conditioning the null on the quantity being tested.

Empirical p-values use the same finite-sample correction as above; BH correction is applied across all tested pathways. This null is less sensitive to bridge edge fraction than the pathway-node rewiring null because it operates on the fixed propagated signal rather than the propagation operator itself. The two nulls are complementary: the pathway-node rewiring null tests whether signal accumulates at the pathway node, while the member-level null tests whether the genes defining that pathway carry disproportionate propagated signal.

5.4 Stability analysis

Statistical significance under the rewiring null establishes that a pathway score exceeds topological expectation for a given seed set. It does not establish whether that score is a stable property of the underlying biology or an artifact of the specific genes provided as input. A pathway that ranks highly and passes significance testing under many different seed configurations is more credible than one that does so only for a particular input.

Stability is assessed by perturbing the seed set and measuring whether pathway recovery is preserved. For gene pools small enough to enumerate completely, all possible seed partitions are evaluated exhaustively. For large cohort gene sets where exhaustive enumeration is not feasible, bootstrap resampling draws random subsets of varying size from the full seed pool. In both cases, the primary question is whether the signal is a property of the biology represented in the gene set or a consequence of the specific input configuration. The implementations of these two approaches are described in the benchmark and cohort sections where they are applied.

Part IV: Benchmark evaluation

6 Benchmark design

6.1 Task types

Two benchmark task types are defined. In a discovery benchmark, seed genes are not members of the target reference pathways; the evaluation tests whether BIFO can identify biologically relevant pathway families from a gene set that does not directly overlap those families. In a recovery benchmark, seed genes are drawn directly from target pathway members; the evaluation tests whether BIFO can recover the source pathway and related pathways from partial membership.

These task types produce systematically different results and must be interpreted separately. In the recovery setting, seed-overlap Fisher enrichment is naturally strong because the overlap between seeds and pathway members is direct and large. In the discovery setting, Fisher enrichment degrades because the overlap is indirect, requiring propagation to bridge the gap between the seed genes and the relevant pathway family. BIFO is designed primarily for the discovery setting, and its advantage over overlap-based methods is largest there.

6.3 Benchmark 1: CHD curated discovery benchmark

The CHD curated benchmark is the primary evaluation of BIFO pathway prioritization performance. It uses ten canonical transcription factor and structural genes from the CHD literature as seeds: GATA4, NKX2-5, TBX5, NOTCH1, NOTCH2, HAND1, HAND2, MYH6, GATA6, and TBX1. These genes were selected based on their established roles in cardiac development and CHD genetics and were not selected to maximize BIFO performance.

Five additional CHD-associated genes (ZFPM2, MYH7, PTPN11, JAG1, FLT4) are designated as held-out nodes. These genes receive no seed mass during propagation and are invisible to the PPR operator. After propagation, their scores are examined to ask whether the 10 seeds, propagating through the conditioned graph, drove score mass toward these 5 biologically relevant genes even though they were not seeded. This is a gene-level recovery test: the held-out genes serve as a positive control for signal localization, distinguishing biologically meaningful propagation from mass accumulating at high-degree hub nodes. Gene-level recovery is measured by AUPRC over the held-out genes versus all other non-seed, non-heldout nodes in the graph.

The pathway reference set consists of 36 literature-curated cardiac pathway CUIs; 18 are present in the scored pathway universe and serve as the pathway-level evaluation reference. Pathway scoring uses all 10 seeds and is evaluated independently of the held-out gene analysis.

Because the seed genes are transcription factors and structural cardiac genes rather than direct pathway members, this is a discovery task: BIFO must propagate signal from the seeds through the conditioned graph and across the bridge layer to reach and prioritize the relevant pathway family. The fixed 10/5 split described here defines the primary benchmark run. The relationship between this configuration and the exhaustive resampling across all possible 10/5 splits of the 15-gene pool is described in Section 9.

The graph for this benchmark was queried as a 1-hop neighborhood centered on the 15 CHD-associated genes, producing edges_raw.csv (94,790 mechanistic and association edges) and pathway_membership_edges.csv (79,562 gene-to-pathway membership edges), merged into edges_merged.csv (174,352 edges total). Node metadata was exported in nodes.csv (34,523 concept nodes). BIFO conditioning resolved 18,897 of 34,523 concept nodes (54.7%) to source vocabularies; the remaining 15,626 structurally peripheral nodes were treated as unresolvable and their edges dropped. The three-arm ablation design applied to this benchmark is described in Section 6.5.

6.4 Benchmarks 2 and 3: C4 pathway-split recovery controls

The C4 controls evaluate BIFO in the recovery setting, where seeds are drawn directly from the members of a known pathway. These benchmarks serve a different purpose from the CHD curated benchmark: they test whether BIFO can recover the source pathway and related pathway family from a partial membership set, and they establish a baseline expectation for rank improvement in the recovery regime.

Two pathway splits are used. C4/Notch uses a 70%/30% random split of REACTOME_SIGNALING_BY_NOTCH (MSigDB M10189; 44 total members), yielding 30 seed genes and 14 held-out genes. This pathway is cardiac-relevant and tests recovery within a coherent, well-annotated signaling family. C4/MAPK uses a 70%/30% random split of REACTOME_MAPK_FAMILY_SIGNALING_CASCADES (MSigDB M27565; 91 total members), yielding 63 seed genes and 28 held-out genes. This pathway is broader and less cohesive than the Notch pathway and is not related to the CHD seed set, providing a test of recovery in a less structured biological context. Both splits used numpy.random.seed(42) and all pathway members were verified to be present in the node index before splitting.

Because seeds in the recovery setting are drawn from pathway members, direct overlap with the reference pathway is high by construction. Fisher enrichment therefore performs well in this setting, and rank improvement relative to raw PPR is expected to be smaller or negative. Results from the C4 controls are reported separately from the CHD curated benchmark and interpreted accordingly.

6.5 Three-arm ablation design

The three-arm ablation design is applied to the CHD curated benchmark to isolate the contribution of specific edge classes to pathway scoring. The PPR operator is varied across three arms while the scoring stage (pathway membership map, pathway universe, and reference set) is held constant. Differences in scoring outcomes are therefore directly attributable to operator composition.

The three arms are constructed from the 105,192 edges retained after Level 2 conditioning of the CHD benchmark graph. The full arm uses all 57,005 propagating edges, including Pathway Contribution bridge edges in the gene-to-pathway direction. The ablation arm conditions the mechanistic and association edges independently of the membership edges, retaining 14,413 propagating edges with no Pathway Contribution bridge edges; this isolates the effect of removing the gene-to-pathway bridge while preserving all other admissible flow classes. The mechanistic-only arm retains only the 9,710 edges classified as strictly mechanistic (Signal Transduction 5,786, Transcription 1,568, Signal Termination 484, and minor mechanistic classes), excluding Pathway Contribution, Observational Association, and all weak-mechanistic edges.

Observational Association edges (9,909), Pathway Contribution edges in the pathway-to-gene direction (37,352), weak-mechanistic Genetic Regulatory Modulation edges (64), and Spatial constraint edges (862) are retained in kept_edges.csv but excluded from all three PPR adjacency matrices. Gene-level analyses additionally include raw propagation and random sparsification controls (Table 2); pathway-level ablation focuses on these three BIFO-derived operators.

7 Baseline enrichment methods

Baselines are designed to represent the strongest approaches a bioinformatician would realistically apply to the same data, not deliberately weak comparators. All baselines are evaluated on the identical pathway universe and reference set as the BIFO arms. When run alongside BIFO-PPR, baselines are additionally restricted to the BIFO-scored pathway universe to ensure direct comparability; pathways not present in the BIFO output are excluded from baseline evaluation.

B0: Degree-weighted seed overlap. For each pathway \(p\), the score is the sum of conditioned-graph out-degrees of seed genes that are direct members of \(p\), normalized by pathway size:

\[\text{score}(p) = \sum_{g \in \text{seeds} \cap \text{members}(p)} \text{degree}_{\text{cond}}(g) \;/\; \sqrt{|\text{members}(p)|}\](4)

This baseline uses graph structure but not propagation: it scores pathways based on how well-connected the directly overlapping seed genes are within the conditioned graph, without running PPR. It isolates the contribution of propagation beyond local connectivity.

B1: Seed-only hypergeometric enrichment. A one-tailed hypergeometric test is applied for each pathway, testing whether seed genes are over-represented among pathway members relative to a background gene universe. P-values are BH-corrected across all pathways. This baseline represents what a bioinformatician would do with the seed gene list alone and no graph information.

B1 is run in two regimes that differ in numerical precision and gene universe definition. The standard regime is appropriate when the seed query is small relative to the gene universe, as in the curated benchmark (10 seeds against approximately 13,000 graph-connected genes); in this regime non-specific hits dominate because a ten-gene query cannot discriminate against a large background, but rank ordering is preserved. The log-space regime is required for the KF cohort analyses (1,276 to 1,395 seeds), where standard-precision hypergeometric computation returns p = 0.0 for every pathway with any meaningful overlap and rank discrimination collapses; the log-space regime restricts the gene universe to pathway-member genes and computes survival probabilities in log space to preserve ranking. The two regimes answer different statistical questions on different gene universes and produce numerically incomparable results. The appropriate regime for each analysis is specified where results are reported. Implementation details, including the scipy functions used in each regime, the relationship between universe size and the K parameter, and the underflow-floor handling, are provided in Supplementary §SM3.1.

B2: 1-hop neighborhood hypergeometric enrichment. The query set is expanded to include all gene-concept neighbors of the seed genes in the merged edge file, and the same hypergeometric test is applied to the expanded query. This represents the natural workflow of extracting a graph neighborhood and running standard enrichment. The neighborhood inflation problem, in which the expanded query covers the majority of the pathway gene universe and eliminates discriminative power, is itself an informative result about the limitations of this approach.

B3 / B3b: Preranked GSEA on PPR scores. Genes are ranked by descending PPR score (raw graph for B3; BIFO-conditioned graph for B3b) and a weighted running-sum enrichment score is computed for each pathway following the preranked GSEA algorithm of Subramanian et al. [15]. The pathway membership map is identical to the one used by BIFO-PPR, drawn from the same MSigDB and WikiPathways pathway concept nodes, so that the gene set universe is matched 1:1 across methods. Pathways are ranked by enrichment score for evaluation against the reference pathway set. B3 tests whether propagation alone recovers pathway-relevant signal without BIFO conditioning. B3b tests whether BIFO-conditioned gene-level scores, used as input to GSEA, improve over raw propagation. The difference between B3b and the BIFO-PPR full arm isolates the contribution of the degree_norm pathway-level scoring function beyond what gene-level score ordering provides. Implementation details, including the from-scratch numpy-based computation, the weighting and miss-penalty parameters, the size filter, and the rationale for not computing a permutation-based null, are provided in Supplementary §SM3.2. ## 8 Evaluation metrics

9 Exhaustive resampling

The CHD curated benchmark evaluates BIFO from a single fixed seed configuration, which raises the question of whether results are specific to that particular gene set. To address this, all C(15,10) = 3,003 possible 10-gene/5-gene partitions of the 15-gene CHD pool are evaluated, exhaustively implementing the stability analysis described in Section 5.4.

The PPR operators and pathway membership map are built once and held in memory. For each of the 3,003 splits only the seed vector is modified; the adjacency matrices, pathway universe, and reference set remain identical to the primary benchmark. Per-split metrics are accumulated in memory and written as a single output file at the end of the run, avoiding thousands of intermediate files.

For each split the following are computed: BIFO-PPR full-arm pathway metrics (P@10, P@20, enrichment@10, NDCG@10, average precision, mean reference pathway rank, and rank improvement); gene-level AUPRC for held-out gene recovery; and a seed-overlap Fisher baseline. The seed-overlap Fisher computed here uses the split seed genes directly as the query set, testing whether those specific genes are over-represented as pathway members. This is distinct from the B1 and B2 baselines in Section 7, which use the full seed set or its graph neighborhood; the two are not numerically comparable and address different questions. The resampling Fisher uses standard-precision hypergeometric computation throughout; query sizes in the resampling regime (n = 10 to 30 split seeds; n = 10 to 30 bootstrap seeds in §15) do not trigger the p-value floor collapse that necessitates the log-space regime in the full-cohort analyses (see Supplementary §SM3.3).

The analysis is parallelized using Python multiprocessing; PPR operator components are serialized to worker processes at startup and each worker processes an assigned batch of splits independently. Results are summarized as distribution statistics across all 3,003 splits, robustness counts, and the primary benchmark split identified as an anchor point within the full distribution. The primary split corresponds to the first 10 genes in the defined CHD pool ordering. The pathway universe for resampling is filtered to the BIFO-scored pathway universe, ensuring direct comparability with the primary benchmark; the gene universe is derived from all C-prefixed nodes in the merged edge file. Membership construction applies the same size and name-pattern filters as the primary scoring pipeline; the BIFO universe restriction provides equivalent coverage of cross-vocabulary exclusions applied by the SAB-aware membership construction in the primary scoring pipeline.

Part V: Kids First cohort application

10 The rare variant enrichment problem

Standard pathway enrichment methods were designed for gene sets produced by differential expression or GWAS association, where input lists are typically small, focused, and biologically coherent. Rare variant cohort analysis occupies a different regime. Aggregating pathogenic and likely pathogenic (P/LP) variants across a disease cohort using AutoGVP [19] typically produces gene lists of hundreds to thousands of genes, with biologically relevant signal distributed across a minority of genes embedded in a larger heterogeneous background.

This creates a fundamental tension for enrichment testing. When carrier frequency filters are strict, the gene list shrinks to tens of genes but becomes dominated by severe recessive disease genes incidentally present in the cohort (lysosomal storage disorders, hereditary hearing loss, retinal dystrophy), obscuring the distributed developmental signal of interest. When filters are relaxed to capture the full P/LP burden, the gene list expands to 1,000 to 1,500 genes, large enough that hypergeometric p-values collapse to zero for virtually every pathway with any overlap, eliminating rank discrimination entirely. As described in Section 7, log-space computation and a restricted gene universe recover correct relative ordering in this regime, but the fundamental problem remains: a gene list of this size and heterogeneity carries weak enrichment structure regardless of implementation.

BIFO addresses this directly. By propagating signal from all seeds simultaneously through the conditioned graph, coherent biological signal from a functionally related gene subset concentrates at relevant pathway nodes while incoherent background signal diffuses away. The approach does not require the input gene set to be small or homogeneous.

11 Cohort and variant selection

Germline variant data were obtained from two Kids First pediatric cohorts: KF-CHD (Pediatric Cardiac Genomics Consortium, phs001138, n=697 probands with congenital heart defects) and KF-NBL (Discovering the Genetic Basis of Human Neuroblastoma, phs001436, n=460 probands with neuroblastoma). Whole-genome sequencing was performed using Kids First harmonization pipelines aligned to GRCh38/GENCODE v39.

Variants were filtered retaining GATK PASS calls with genotype quality ≥20 and read depth ≥10. AutoGVP P/LP classification was applied integrating ClinVar and modified InterVar for hierarchical ACMG-AMP criteria assessment. Population allele frequency filtering used gnomAD v3.1 [20] at MAF ≤0.001, matching the companion U24 cross-cohort enrichment analysis (Stear et al., CFDE Meeting 2026). Genes harboring at least one qualifying variant in any proband were aggregated per cohort.

Twenty high-frequency background disease genes were excluded: genes among the most prevalent recessive carrier loci in population-scale sequencing (carrier frequency >1% in gnomAD) that represent incidental findings unrelated to the disease of interest. The excluded genes encode proteins associated with hereditary hearing loss (GJB2, MYO15A, MYO1A, MYO3A, MYO7A, USH2A), retinal dystrophy (ABCA4), skin barrier dysfunction (FLG, KRT71, KRT86, TCHH, PADI3), metabolic disease (G6PD, PAH, BCHE), and structural or connective tissue disorders (TTN, OBSCN, COL4A5, TBL1Y, CD36). This yielded 1,287 seed genes for KF-CHD and 1,406 seed genes for KF-NBL.

At the MAF ≤0.001, n≥1 threshold, 570 of 1,287 CHD seed genes (44.3%) are also present in the NBL seed set. This inter-cohort overlap reflects the shared background of Mendelian disease gene carriers present in any pediatric sequencing cohort at this allele frequency threshold and is not specific to either disease. Carrier count-filtered seed sets (n≥2: 387 CHD / 401 NBL genes, 30% overlap; n≥3: 146 CHD / 147 NBL genes, 27% overlap) show reduced but persistent overlap, consistent with shared rare variant burden being a property of the population rather than the disease.

12 Graph export and conditioning

Export queries were generated dynamically from each seed list. The KF-CHD export produced 815,248 concept nodes and 5,261,300 1-hop edges plus 486,642 gene-to-pathway membership edges; the KF-NBL export produced 880,456 concept nodes and similar edge counts. An additional export provided node metadata for 21,353 MSigDB pathway member genes not present in the 1-hop seed neighborhood, required for complete pathway membership resolution. Of 1,287 CHD seed genes, 1,276 (99.1%) resolved to UMLS CUIs; of 1,406 NBL seed genes, 1,395 (99.2%) resolved. BIFO conditioning used identical parameters to the curated benchmark (alpha=0.5, bifo_mapping.yaml v0.7.1), retaining 2,482,752 propagating edges for KF-CHD (43.9% of concept edges) and 2,647,055 for KF-NBL (45.1%).

13 Pathway scoring and reference set

Pathway scoring used degree_norm against a universe of 2,130 pathways for KF-CHD and 2,196 for KF-NBL, each passing the size, name-pattern, and canonical-collection filters described in Section 4.1. Canonical collections retained were Hallmarks, Reactome, WikiPathways, KEGG, BioCarta, and PID; C2.CGP author-named gene expression signature sets were excluded. All pathways were scored and ranked in discovery mode with no reference pathway set pre-specified. Full pathway-level scoring results for both cohorts, including per-pathway seed gene membership, PPR-normalized member scores, and influential node z-score summaries, are provided as Supplementary Data SD1 (KF-CHD) and SD2 (KF-NBL). Structured input documents for LLM-assisted biological interpretation of these results, including column definitions, analytical task instructions, and provenance records, are provided as Supplementary Tables ST8 (KF-CHD) and ST9 (KF-NBL); verbatim LLM responses are in Supplementary Section S1.

Of 17 candidate reference pathways, 16 were present in the scored universe after canonical-collection filtering; this 16-pathway subset is used for all quantitative metrics. A broader 21-pathway expanded reference set including additional GO and Reactome cilia annotations is used for visualization in Figure 4 only.

14 Baseline comparisons and rank analysis

The same baseline methods described in Section 7 are applied to the KF cohort analyses using the cohort-appropriate settings: log-space hypergeometric computation and a gene universe restricted to pathway member genes (excluding seed genes), as described in Section 7 (B1). All methods are evaluated on identical pathway universes to enable direct rank comparison.

The primary comparison metric is the rank of WP_CILIOPATHIES and other cilia-related pathways under each method in full discovery mode. Secondary comparisons examine the top-20 ranked pathways per method, the head-to-head rank correlation between Fisher enrichment and BIFO across all pathways, and the full cilia pathway cluster ranking under BIFO-PPR. Concordance between BIFO and correctly implemented Fisher enrichment on the top cilia hit is reported as independent validation of the biological signal rather than a competitive comparison, for the reasons described in Section 6.1.

15 Bootstrap resampling

Stability of cilia pathway recovery across seed set sizes is assessed by bootstrap resampling, implementing the approach described in Section 5.4 for the large cohort setting where exhaustive enumeration is not feasible. For each of three seed sizes (n=10, 20, 30), 500 bootstrap draws were made by sampling uniformly without replacement from the full seed CUI pool. For each draw, BIFO scoring and seed-only Fisher enrichment were run using the identical pathway universe and cilia reference set as the primary analysis.

Performance was measured as Precision@10 against the 16-pathway cilia reference, average precision, and rank improvement. The full cohort run (1,276 seeds for KF-CHD; 1,395 for KF-NBL) was included as a reference point. All 1,500 bootstrap runs per cohort were parallelized across 192 cores; total runtime was approximately 150 seconds per cohort.

The bootstrap analysis addresses two questions: whether cilia pathway recovery requires the full cohort-scale variant burden or can emerge from small random gene subsets; and whether BIFO or Fisher enrichment is more sensitive for detecting cilia signal at reduced seed sizes. At bootstrap seed sizes of 10 to 30 genes against a pathway-member universe of approximately 22,600 genes, p-value floor collapse does not occur and standard-precision hypergeometric computation is sufficient. The gene universe consists of pathway-connected genes derived from membership edges in the merged edge file. The bootstrap uses the same BIFO-filtered pathway universe as the primary cohort analysis; membership construction applies the same size and name-pattern filters as the primary scoring pipeline, with SAB-constraint differences documented in Methods §9.

Results

BIFO conditioning was evaluated across three controlled benchmarks and two independent pediatric rare variant cohorts. In the curated CHD benchmark, BIFO conditioning of a controlled DDKG graph projection produced a structured propagation substrate in which pathway nodes are reachable only through a formally defined bridge edge class; a three-arm ablation design established that this bridge layer is structurally necessary for pathway-level signal transfer. BIFO achieved P@10 = 0.70 against an 18-pathway CHD reference, outperforming all standard enrichment baselines in the curated CHD benchmark comparison, and the result was stable across all 3,003 possible seed configurations of the 15-gene CHD pool. Pathway-split recovery controls showed that the method generalizes beyond disease-gene curation. Applied to two independent Kids First rare variant cohorts, BIFO-PPR recovered a coherent ciliopathy pathway cluster in both congenital heart disease and neuroblastoma in full discovery mode, with WP_CILIOPATHIES carrying one of the strongest statistical enrichment signals among well-calibrated pathways in KF-CHD (null_z = 48.7, q = 0.008, rank 43 of 2,130 by degree_norm; rank 11 of 2,019 by null_z) and ranking third in KF-NBL (null_z = 19.0, q = 0.012). Correctly implemented Fisher enrichment independently ranked WP_CILIOPATHIES first in both cohorts, supporting that the recovered signal reflects underlying biology rather than a propagation artifact. Bootstrap resampling established that this signal requires aggregate cohort-scale variant burden and is not recoverable from small random gene subsets. Together, these results test whether BIFO’s ontology-defined constraints on information flow produce measurable inference benefits when implemented as BIFO-PPR.

3.3 Mechanistic-only arm

Restricting propagation to strictly mechanistic edges (9,710 edges: Signal Transduction 5,786, Transcription 1,568, Signal Termination 484, and minor mechanistic classes) produced exactly zero scores across all 563 pathways (P@10 = 0.00). This is a structural result, not a performance result. No amount of mechanistic propagation can produce pathway scores in this graph representation without them. The significance of this finding for knowledge graph architecture more broadly is discussed in the Discussion.

3.4 Two-layer graph architecture

Together, the three arms identify a two-layer graph architecture with a structurally necessary bridge. The molecular layer encodes gene-gene signaling, transcription, and protein interaction through mechanistic edges. The pathway layer encodes curated gene-set annotations. These layers are structurally separated in this graph; direct connectivity between them is mediated exclusively by Pathway Contribution edges. BIFO’s contribution is the principled, ontology-aligned admission of those bridge edges as a coherent propagation-eligible flow class. Without formally recognizing and admitting them, propagation operating on mechanistic edges alone cannot reach pathway concept nodes in this graph construction and scoring formulation.

This architectural property is a consequence of how curated pathway memberships are represented in the DDKG, not an artifact of incomplete edge coverage. Whether it persists under expanded SAB selections incorporating additional evidence layers remains an open question; the present result establishes it for the ontology-aligned mechanistic and pathway representation used here.

Scope: these conclusions apply to the present benchmark graph, constructed from a defined SAB subset that isolates ontology-aligned mechanistic and pathway representations from association-derived evidence layers.

Neighborhood Fisher (B2) failed entirely, producing P@10 = 0.000 and AP = 0.036. The 1-hop gene neighborhood around the ten seed genes contained 11,146 genes, which is 86% of the full gene universe. At that coverage, virtually every pathway has substantial neighborhood overlap, and p-values are near-identical across pathways, eliminating all rank discrimination. This illustrates a fundamental problem with graph-derived gene sets: expanding to a neighborhood before enrichment testing inflates the query to the point where the test loses all power.

Raw PPR GSEA (B3) recovered BRUNEAU_SEPTATION_VENTRICULAR at rank 1 (P@10 = 0.10, AP = 0.117), showing that graph propagation carries pathway-relevant signal even before BIFO conditioning. However, gene-level PPR scores used as input to GSEA are not specific enough to rank more than one or two reference pathways highly; the method does not distinguish between pathways that accumulate signal because their member genes are biologically relevant and pathways that accumulate signal simply because their member genes are well-connected hubs. Conditioning PPR GSEA (B3b, AP = 0.088) shows that applying BIFO conditioning to the gene-level scores before GSEA does not substantially improve this, because the bottleneck is the gene-to-pathway scoring step rather than the propagation itself. The improvement from B3b (AP = 0.088) to BIFO-PPR full-arm (AP = 0.408) therefore isolates the specific contribution of scoring pathway nodes directly via the bridge layer rather than aggregating gene-level scores.

Taken together, standard enrichment methods fail at both ends of the gene set size spectrum for this benchmark: seed Fisher is unstable with ten genes, and neighborhood Fisher loses all discriminating power with 11,000. BIFO avoids both failure modes by concentrating signal through conditioned propagation before scoring, and by scoring pathway nodes directly rather than through gene-level intermediates.

5 Recovery controls: C4 pathway-split benchmarks

The CHD curated benchmark is a discovery task: seed genes are transcription factors and structural cardiac genes that do not directly overlap the target pathway family. To evaluate BIFO independently of disease-gene curation and in a setting where the answer is known by construction, two recovery benchmarks were added. In a recovery benchmark, seeds are drawn directly from the members of a known pathway, and the correct answer (that pathway and its family) is specified before the analysis runs. This design tests a different question from the CHD benchmark: not whether BIFO can find a biologically relevant pathway from disease genes, but whether it can correctly identify the source pathway when the seeds are already members of it. Because the seed genes overlap substantially with the target pathway by construction, the raw PPR arm already has direct proximity to the target, and negative rank improvement is expected. The relevant metrics in this setting are whether the source pathway ranks first and how coherently the broader pathway family is recovered.

Notch signaling was chosen as the first recovery control because it is a well-defined, biologically coherent pathway with clear cardiac relevance: Notch pathway mutations are among the best-characterized causes of congenital heart defects, making it a meaningful test of whether BIFO can recover a pathway family that is directly connected to the disease biology used in the CHD benchmark. 5.2 C4/MAPK: recovering a broader, less cohesive pathway family

The MAPK control was designed to test BIFO under harder recovery conditions: MAPK signaling cascades form a broader, more heterogeneous family than Notch, with membership spread across many functionally distinct processes. Unlike the Notch control, which tests recovery within a tight, well-annotated signaling family with direct cardiac relevance, the MAPK control asks whether BIFO can recover a pathway family that is structurally diffuse and biologically unrelated to the CHD seed set. If BIFO is sensitive to pathway family cohesion in the way we expect, performance here should be weaker than in the Notch control.

Seeds were 63 genes from REACTOME_MAPK_FAMILY_SIGNALING_CASCADES (91 total members), with 28 held out. BIFO-PPR ranked the source pathway first, recovering it correctly, but P@10 = 0.10 and AP = 0.174, substantially weaker than the Notch control. A P@10 of 0.10 means only 1 of the top 10 ranked pathways belonged to the MAPK reference family, reflecting the structural diffuseness of the MAPK family across the graph. Neighborhood Fisher achieved P@10 = 0.10 with AP = 0.164, a rare case where neighborhood expansion is partially competitive. This occurs because MAPK pathway connectivity is partially accessible through the immediate graph neighborhood, unlike the CHD discovery task where neighborhood expansion inflates the query to the full gene universe and eliminates all discriminating power. BIFO’s modest AP advantage (0.174 vs. 0.164) is consistent with the two methods drawing on overlapping information when the target pathway is structurally accessible without propagation.

5.3 Interpretation

In both C4 controls BIFO correctly identifies the source pathway at rank 1 regardless of pathway family cohesion. The contrast between C4/Notch and C4/MAPK is itself informative: tightly defined pathway families with coherent graph structure are recovered more completely than broad, heterogeneous ones, and this difference scales with biological cohesion rather than seed set size (C4/MAPK had more seeds than C4/Notch but lower recovery). Together with the CHD discovery benchmark, these results show that BIFO operates consistently across both task types, with its largest advantage over standard methods in the discovery setting where propagation must bridge the gap between seed genes and a pathway family that does not directly overlap them.

7 Kids First cohort analysis: discovery-mode pathway recovery

Applied to two independent pediatric rare variant cohorts from the Kids First program, BIFO-PPR recovered WP_CILIOPATHIES as a top-ranked pathway in both congenital heart disease and neuroblastoma in full discovery mode, without any pre-specification of a cilia reference. In KF-CHD, WP_CILIOPATHIES ranked 43rd of 2,130 pathways with one of the strongest statistical enrichment signals among well-calibrated pathways (null_z = 48.7, q = 0.008; rank 11 of 2,019 calibrated pathways by null_z). In KF-NBL, it ranked 3rd of 2,196 pathways (null_z = 19.0, q = 0.012). Correctly implemented Fisher enrichment ranked WP_CILIOPATHIES first in both cohorts independently. The convergence of two methods across two biologically distinct diseases, using independent variant sets, supports an underlying biological signal rather than a propagation artifact.

7.1 The enrichment challenge at cohort scale

Rare variant aggregation across a disease cohort typically produces gene lists of 1,000 to 1,500 genes, far larger than the gene sets standard enrichment methods were designed for. At this scale, hypergeometric p-values collapse to near-zero floor values for a large fraction of pathways, compressing rank discrimination. As described in Methods Section 10, this problem is not unique to naive implementations: even with correct log-space computation, a gene list of this size and heterogeneity carries distributed enrichment structure because the biologically relevant signal spans a small subset of genes embedded in a large background of incidental carriers. Fisher identifies the top-hit overlap when the signal is strong enough – as it does for WP_CILIOPATHIES in both KF cohorts – but does not provide pathway-level statistical specificity (distinguishing signal from graph-topology artifacts) or coherent cluster recovery across the full cilia pathway family. BIFO-PPR adds these through the rewiring null and pathway-node scoring, which are structurally independent of direct gene-pathway overlap and therefore provide complementary rather than redundant evidence.

BIFO-PPR addresses this by propagating signal from all seeds simultaneously through the conditioned graph. Coherent biological signal from a functionally related gene subset, such as the 22 cilia-associated genes in the KF-CHD seed pool, concentrates at relevant pathway nodes while incoherent background signal from the remaining 1,254 genes diffuses away. The approach does not require the input gene set to be small or homogeneous.

7.2 KF-CHD: ciliopathy signal in congenital heart disease

The KF-CHD analysis used 1,276 resolved seed genes from 697 probands with congenital heart defects, scored against a universe of 2,130 pathways in full discovery mode. No reference pathway set was pre-specified; all pathways were scored and ranked, and results were evaluated post-hoc against a 16-pathway cilia reference set.

BIFO-PPR results. WP_CILIOPATHIES ranked 43rd of 2,130 pathways by degree_norm (degree_norm = 8.51×10⁻⁶), placing it in the top 2% of the scored universe. WP_JOUBERT_SYNDROME, a closely related ciliopathy pathway sharing many structural members, ranked 34th. Statistical enrichment. The pathway-node rewiring null establishes that WP_CILIOPATHIES carries one of the strongest statistical signals in the calibrated set: null_z = 48.7 (q = 0.008; rank 11 of 2,019 calibrated pathways by null_z), meaning the observed degree_norm score is 48.7 standard deviations above the mean of 1,000 degree-preserving membership rewiring permutations. This directly tests whether the specific gene membership of WP_CILIOPATHIES drives the signal, rather than graph topology alone. The member-level null (member_mean null_z = 1.39, p = 0.057) indicates that signal is concentrated primarily at the pathway node rather than distributed uniformly across all 170 member genes, consistent with a subset of cilia-associated variant genes driving mass to the ciliopathy cluster through the mechanistic network.

Degree_norm and null_z are complementary measures rather than interchangeable rankings. Degree_norm provides absolute prioritization across all pathways; null_z tests statistical specificity relative to a graph-specific null. WP_CILIOPATHIES ranks 43rd by degree_norm because large, highly connected hub pathways accumulate more absolute propagated signal, but it ranks first by null_z because its signal is specifically attributable to its gene membership rather than to graph structure. Both pieces of evidence are needed: rank alone does not establish specificity, and null_z alone does not establish that the pathway scores above the bulk of the universe.

Of 16 matched cilia, ciliopathy, and related pathway annotations in the scored universe, 8 (50%) rank in the top half. Five distinct cilia-cluster pathways are therefore among the top 1% of the calibrated set by null_z. The cilia signal in KF-CHD is therefore specific to ciliary structure and basal body function rather than reflecting broad Hedgehog transcriptional programs.

7.3 KF-NBL: independent replication in neuroblastoma

The KF-NBL analysis used 1,395 resolved seed genes from 460 probands with neuroblastoma, scored against a universe of 2,196 pathways. The cohort is biologically distinct from KF-CHD: neuroblastoma is a tumor of neural crest origin, and the KF-NBL seed set contains a prominent DNA repair component (ATM, ATR, ATRX, BARD1, BLM, BRCA2, BRIP1, APTX) not present in the CHD cohort. The question is whether BIFO-PPR recovers the same ciliopathy signal in a different disease context using an independent variant set.

WP_CILIOPATHIES ranked 3rd of 2,196 pathways by degree_norm (degree_norm = 4.24×10⁻⁶, null_z = 19.0, q = 0.012), with WP_JOUBERT_SYNDROME ranking 17th. The top two pathways by degree_norm were GO terms reflecting broader membrane transport biology (Ionophore activity, Transmembrane Transport), consistent with the broader neuronal character of the NBL seed neighborhood. Seed-only Fisher ranked WP_CILIOPATHIES first of 2,196 pathways (BH p = 3.85×10⁻³²), replicating the KF-CHD Fisher result in an independent cohort.

The member-level null for KF-NBL shows significant signal (member_mean null_z = 2.43, p = 0.003), in contrast to KF-CHD where member-level signal did not reach significance (p = 0.057). This difference is consistent with the cilia signal being more distributed across pathway member genes in NBL than in CHD, reflecting a different composition of cilia-associated variant genes between the two cohorts rather than a stronger overall signal. The pathway-node null_z in KF-NBL (19.0) is lower than in KF-CHD (48.7), consistent with WP_CILIOPATHIES ranking higher by degree_norm in NBL (rank 3 vs. rank 43) and therefore having a smaller gap between its observed score and the scores of hub pathways above it.

7.4 Cross-cohort convergence

The choice of these two particular cohorts is not arbitrary. Children with congenital heart disease have elevated neuroblastoma risk, potentially due to shared neural crest developmental origins [14], and children with neural crest-origin birth defects including selected congenital heart defects are approximately three times as likely to be diagnosed with neuroblastoma compared to children without a birth defect [21]. GWAS analyses have identified shared genetic architecture between neuroblastoma and ventricular septal defect, with shared susceptibility loci regulating genes involved in neural crest cell formation including BARD1, MSX1, and SHOX2 [22]. The epidemiological and genetic co-occurrence of these two diseases frames our cross-cohort comparison not as two unrelated diseases that happen to share a pathway hit, but as two manifestations of a shared developmental vulnerability in neural crest-derived tissues. Primary cilia are required for normal neural crest cell migration, cardiac septation, and left-right asymmetry, making them a biologically plausible shared mechanism. The convergent recovery of WP_CILIOPATHIES under both BIFO-PPR and correctly implemented Fisher enrichment in independent variant sets from both diseases is therefore consistent with a shared biological signal rather than coincidence.

No method recovers it consistently from 10 to 30 randomly drawn genes. This is itself a biological result: it means the signal is not driven by a single highly penetrant gene or a small cluster of genes, but by the collective contribution of many cilia-associated variant carriers across the cohort. BIFO-PPR’s graph propagation and pathway-node scoring provide an independent view of that distributed signal, complementary to the direct overlap enrichment that Fisher uses to identify the strongest individual hit.

Discussion

BIFO provides a formalism for functional inference from heterogeneous biomedical knowledge graphs, addressing two compounding problems that limit biological discovery in this setting. First, when mechanistic relationships, statistical associations, and annotation edges share a single graph structure, propagation treats them equivalently, making biologically meaningful signal indistinguishable from incidental connectivity. Second, standard enrichment methods fail at both extremes of the gene set sizes that modern genomics produces: small sets generate statistically unstable overlap, and large heterogeneous sets from rare variant aggregation cause p-value floor collapse or neighborhood inflation that eliminates all discriminating power. These problems compound in rare variant cohort analysis, where gene sets are large, signal is distributed, and the biological questions are most consequential. BIFO resolves the first problem by restricting propagation to edges that encode directed biological transformations and defining a formally distinct bridge class connecting the molecular layer to pathway annotations. It addresses the second by evaluating pathway-level signal at pathway concept nodes rather than through gene-level aggregation, remaining effective in regimes where standard methods lose discriminative power or pathway-level statistical specificity. In this paper we evaluate the framework in a gene-to-pathway inference setting using rare variant cohort data, but the underlying formalism applies to any feature-to-feature inference problem on a heterogeneous biological graph: drug-target inference, phenotype-to-pathway mapping, cross-species signal transfer, or any setting where the identifiability of causal from associative relationships limits inference.

A natural question is whether this complexity is necessary. If the goal is gene-to-pathway inference, why use a large heterogeneous knowledge graph rather than a simpler local network of genes and pathways? The ablation results provide a direct empirical answer. Removing mechanistic edges entirely (ablation arm, no bridge edges) still recovers pathway signal at P@10 = 0.60, but the full mechanistic-plus-bridge operator achieves P@10 = 0.70. The mechanistic layer contributes real signal: it structures the propagation substrate in a way that concentrates signal within biologically coherent gene neighborhoods before it transfers to the pathway layer through bridge edges. A flat gene-pathway bipartite graph, equivalent to running enrichment directly on pathway membership without propagation, would recover only what direct overlap provides, which is what the baseline comparisons show. The advantage of a richer graph is not complexity for its own sake but the ability to capture biological context that is not encoded in pathway membership alone. BIFO conditioning is what converts that complexity from a liability into an asset, by restricting traversal to biologically valid paths and preventing associative edges from diluting mechanistic signal.

A related question concerns the choice of graph substrate. For example, protein-protein interaction (PPI) networks such as STRING are present in the native DDKG but did not enter the propagating edge set used for the analyses reported here. This is because the 1-hop seed-centered Cypher exports used in this paper are rooted at HGNC gene concept nodes; PPI edges connect protein concept nodes (UniProtKB <-> UniProtKB) and therefore sit one additional hop beyond the export boundary, reachable from gene seeds only via the gene_product_of bridge into the protein layer. As a result, no PPI edges were available to BIFO conditioning in either the curated benchmark or the KF cohort runs, regardless of how the conditioning rules would have classified them. PPI-derived predicates such as interacts_with and molecularly_interacts_with are mapped in bifo_mapping.yaml and would be admitted as Protein Interaction edges if they appeared in the input graph; whether expanding the export to two hops, or rooting the seed query at protein concept nodes, and admitting undirected PPI edges (STRING and others) into the propagation substrate improves pathway recovery is an open question that planned observational edge-augmented analyses will address directly.

The two-layer graph architecture

The three-arm ablation reveals something about the structure of ontology-aligned biomedical knowledge graphs that extends beyond this benchmark. In the present DDKG export, pathway nodes are structurally inaccessible from gene nodes through mechanistic edges alone. Signal propagating through gene-gene regulatory relationships, transcription, protein interactions, and disease-gene associations has no path to pathway concept nodes without bridge edges. This is not a gap in the mechanistic coverage of the graph. It is a consequence of how curated pathway knowledge is represented: pathway membership is encoded as a distinct relationship type that connects the molecular layer to the pathway annotation layer, and that relationship type occupies a structurally separate role from mechanistic gene-gene relationships.

This two-layer architecture has a practical implication for any graph-based inference method operating on similar representations. Propagation methods that do not explicitly admit bridge edges as a propagation-eligible class will produce zero pathway scores regardless of how rich the mechanistic layer is. The contribution of BIFO is not to add information to the graph but to make the admissibility decision explicit: bridge edges are formally defined as a propagation-eligible class, signal flows unidirectionally from gene nodes to pathway nodes, and the conditioning operator encodes this asymmetry in the adjacency matrix. Without this explicit admission, the two layers remain structurally disconnected for propagation purposes even when the edges connecting them are present in the graph.

The mechanistic-only arm establishes this result directly. It is not intended as a competitive baseline. Its purpose is to show that the zero-score outcome is structural, which is what justifies BIFO’s admission of bridge edges as a formally defined class rather than treating them as an implementation detail. The result raises an apparent puzzle: if mechanistic-only propagation yields zero pathway scores, mechanistic edges might appear to contribute nothing to pathway inference. The full ablation resolves this. Mechanistic edges concentrate signal within biologically coherent gene neighborhoods before it transfers to the pathway layer through bridge edges, contributing a measurable improvement in pathway recovery (P@10 = 0.60 vs. 0.70 without vs. with bridge edges in the full arm). The mechanistic layer does not reach pathway nodes directly, but it shapes the signal that bridge edges ultimately transfer.

Propagation and scoring as distinct contributions

The baseline comparisons isolate two separable contributions to pathway recovery performance. The first is signal concentration through conditioned propagation. The second is pathway-level scoring via direct accumulation at pathway concept nodes rather than aggregation of gene-level scores.

Conditioned PPR GSEA (B3b) uses BIFO-conditioned gene-level scores as input to a faithful from-scratch implementation of the preranked GSEA algorithm (Methods §7) on the same pathway membership universe used by BIFO-PPR, achieving AP = 0.088. BIFO-PPR full arm achieves AP = 0.408 on the same conditioned graph. The difference isolates the contribution of the degree_norm scoring function: scoring pathway nodes directly via the bridge layer is substantially more effective than aggregating conditioned gene-level scores through GSEA. This is because GSEA operates on a ranked gene list and does not require signal to accumulate at pathway concept nodes: it can find enrichment through any combination of member gene ranks. BIFO-PPR requires signal to travel through the bridge layer and arrive at the pathway node, which is more demanding but more specific. Pathways that score highly under BIFO-PPR have received propagated signal specifically because their member genes are connected to the seed neighborhood through admissible paths. Pathways that score highly under GSEA may do so because their member genes happen to rank well in the gene-level score distribution for reasons unrelated to pathway-specific signal.

The degree_norm normalization contributes a second specificity gain. Large, highly connected pathways accumulate more propagated signal simply by having more member genes contributing bridge edge mass to the pathway node. The square root penalty down-weights this size effect without fully normalizing it away, preserving signal in pathways that are both large and biologically coherent while suppressing generic hub pathways. The rank vs. null_z landscape in Figure 8 illustrates the result: degree_norm rank and null_z are complementary rather than redundant. Pathways at the top of the degree_norm ranking but with null_z near zero are hub pathways whose scores reflect graph topology. WP_CILIOPATHIES ranks 43rd by degree_norm but carries one of the largest null_z values (48.7) in the calibrated set in KF-CHD, indicating that its signal is specifically attributable to its gene membership rather than to its size or connectivity.

The KF cohort finding and its biological interpretation

The recovery of WP_CILIOPATHIES as the top statistically enriched pathway in both KF-CHD and KF-NBL is consistent with shared developmental biology rather than a methodological artifact, for three independent reasons. First, the signal is recovered by two methods with fundamentally different mechanisms: BIFO-PPR through graph propagation and Fisher enrichment through direct gene-pathway overlap. Their concordance on the same top pathway supports that the result reflects the underlying gene set rather than propagation-specific behavior. Second, the signal requires aggregate cohort-scale variant burden: bootstrap resampling shows that neither method recovers cilia pathways reliably from 10 to 30 randomly drawn genes, establishing that the signal is distributed across many variant carriers rather than driven by a single gene or small cluster. Third, the cross-cohort convergence is biologically motivated: children with congenital heart disease have elevated neuroblastoma risk due to shared neural crest developmental origins [14], and shared genetic architecture between neuroblastoma and ventricular septal defect has been identified at loci regulating neural crest cell formation [22]. Primary cilia are required for neural crest cell migration, cardiac septation, and left-right asymmetry, making them a mechanistically coherent shared vulnerability across both diseases.

The distinction between degree_norm rank and null_z is biologically important in interpreting the KF results. WP_CILIOPATHIES ranks 43rd in KF-CHD, placing it in the top 2% of 2,130 pathways, but it does not appear in the top 10. The top-ranked pathways by degree_norm include broad hub terms (protein phosphorylation, transmembrane transport, cross-presentation) that accumulate high propagated signal through graph connectivity rather than specific biological relevance to the seed set. WP_CILIOPATHIES has one of the largest null_z values among well-calibrated pathways (48.7, ranking 11th of 2,019 by null_z), meaning its score is 48.7 standard deviations above what is expected from degree-preserving random rewiring of its membership. This establishes that the cilia signal is specific to the gene membership of WP_CILIOPATHIES rather than to its position in the graph. In KF-NBL, WP_CILIOPATHIES ranks 3rd by degree_norm and also carries one of the largest null_z values (19.0), suggesting the NBL seed neighborhood has stronger direct cilia connectivity than the CHD neighborhood, consistent with the neural crest origin of neuroblastoma.

To illustrate the exploratory biological interpretation use case enabled by structured BIFO-PPR outputs, the complete scored pathway results for both KF cohorts, including per-pathway seed gene membership sorted by PPR score, PPR-normalized member scores, influential node z-score summaries (local and global), and a structured analytical task framework, were provided to two large language models (Claude Opus 4.7 and GPT-5.3) in isolated chat sessions.

LLM-based interpretation is not part of the BIFO-PPR pipeline and plays no role in the computation of pathway scores, ranking, or statistical significance. It represents a downstream, user-driven analysis step with BIFO-PPR structured output files. These interpretations are provided verbatim and are not edited or optimized, in order to preserve transparency of the interaction. In these sessions, we requested that the LLMs evaluate each cohort as independent evaluations without using other chats as reference.

Both GPT and Claude models independently identified ciliopathy signal as an important theme in both CHD and NBL cohorts, identified DNA repair burden as a secondary theme in KF-NBL, flagged non-obvious network driver genes (E2F1, CD36) in KF-CHD, and issued appropriate skeptical caveats about single-gene-dominated pathways and pathway redundancy. This indicates that BIFO-PPR outputs are structured in a way that supports automated or semi-automated biological interpretation, without requiring the method itself to incorporate any learned model.

LLM interpretations are prompt- and model-dependent and are included for illustrative purposes only, not as reproducible or statistical evidence. The structured output format and analytical task instructions are described in Supplementary Tables ST8 and ST9; verbatim LLM responses are in Supplementary Section S1.

The Hedgehog depletion finding adds biological precision. Generic Hedgehog signaling pathways show negative null_z values in KF-CHD, indicating depletion rather than enrichment, while cilia-specific pathway terms (basal body anchoring, cilium assembly, intraflagellar transport) show strong positive null_z values. This specificity, with cilia structure and trafficking enriched and broad Hedgehog transcriptional programs depleted, is consistent with the known biology: primary cilia are the cellular compartment in which Hedgehog signaling is transduced, but the developmental vulnerability in these cohorts appears to map to the structural and trafficking functions of the cilium rather than to downstream Hedgehog transcriptional output.

Relationship to standard methods

BIFO-PPR is not a replacement for standard enrichment methods. It is most useful in settings where those methods lose specificity or coherent pathway-cluster recovery: when gene sets are large and heterogeneous, when relevant pathways are connected to seed genes indirectly through biological relationships rather than through direct membership overlap, and when the goal is discovery rather than confirmation of a pre-specified pathway family. In the curated CHD benchmark, correctly implemented Fisher enrichment achieves P@10 = 0.30, a meaningful result that identifies three of the top ten pathways correctly. BIFO-PPR improves on this substantially (P@10 = 0.70), but Fisher is not failing; it is operating at the limits of what direct overlap can provide with ten seed genes. In the KF cohort analyses, correctly implemented Fisher independently ranks WP_CILIOPATHIES first in both cohorts. BIFO-PPR and Fisher converge on the same top result through different mechanisms, which strengthens rather than undermines the biological conclusion.

The regime where BIFO-PPR provides the largest practical advantage is the large rare variant cohort setting, where standard enrichment collapses numerically under standard-precision computation and provides weaker statistical specificity and pathway-cluster coherence than graph-based approaches. At 1,276 to 1,395 seed genes, hypergeometric p-values collapse to zero for virtually every pathway with any overlap under standard-precision computation. Correctly implemented log-space Fisher recovers the top cilia hit in both cohorts but provides less pathway-cluster specificity and lacks a null model for distinguishing signal from graph-topology artifacts. BIFO-PPR addresses this by propagating signal from all seeds simultaneously through the conditioned graph, concentrating the coherent biological contribution of the cilia-associated gene subset at relevant pathway nodes while the incoherent background diffuses away. This is not the only setting where BIFO-PPR would be useful: any feature-to-feature inference problem on a heterogeneous graph with the same identifiability challenge would benefit from the same conditioning approach, but it is the setting where the advantage over standard methods is most clearly visible in this paper.

Graph substrate and source vocabulary selection

The analyses reported here used a specific DDKG export that integrates ontology-aligned mechanistic and pathway sources while excluding association-derived evidence layers (age-stratified gene-disease associations, clinical mutation associations, genomic variant associations). This exclusion was deliberate: isolating mechanistic and pathway representations from association-derived layers enables clean attribution of pathway score changes to specific edge classes in the ablation design. Had association layers been included, the mechanistic-only arm result would have been less interpretable. The DDKG was chosen because its predicate-typed edges support the flow-class classification that BIFO conditioning requires; the same conditioning logic applies to any property graph whose edges carry compatible predicate annotations.

The propagating edge set in the benchmark analysis draws from WikiPathways gene-gene interactions, GO biological process annotations, HGNC and GENCODE gene-product relationships, OMIM and ClinGen disease-gene associations, LINCS drug-target correlations, and MSigDB and WikiPathways pathway membership edges. Notably absent are GTEx coexpression edges, which were exported in the 1-hop neighborhood but correctly classified as observational by BIFO conditioning and excluded from propagation.

The full attribution of propagating edges to source vocabularies (which SABs contributed which predicates at what counts) is provided in Supplementary Table ST6. This characterization is relevant to reproducibility: results may differ on DDKG versions with different SAB selections, and the edge composition of the propagating operator is a determinant of pathway scoring outcomes. Future analyses incorporating additional source vocabularies, including STRING, GTEx eQTLs, and expanded disease-gene association layers, will require re-evaluation of BIFO flow class assignments for the new predicate types introduced.

Null calibration and statistical validity

BIFO-PPR pathway scores are made actionable by the membership rewiring null, which tests whether a pathway’s observed score exceeds what is expected given the propagated signal landscape. A finding that emerges from these analyses is that statistical validity in graph-based inference depends not only on the scoring function but on the structure of the propagation operator itself. When bridge edges dominate the propagating graph, rewiring effectively randomizes the dominant routing structure and the null distribution inflates, reducing the discriminative power of the significance test. When non-bridge mechanistic edges provide sufficient routing constraint, rewired bridge assignments that do not match the biological structure of the seed set produce low pathway scores, and the null is well-separated from observed scores.

In the benchmark (76.7% bridge edges), the null is valid because with only 10 seeds, random rewirings rarely replicate specific cardiac signal regardless of bridge fraction. In KF-CHD (41.4% bridge edges under the corrected unidirectional pipeline), sufficient non-bridge routing constraint exists for valid null calibration, and WP_CILIOPATHIES achieves one of the largest null_z values in the calibrated set (48.7, q = 0.008). The same holds for KF-NBL (null_z = 19.0, q = 0.012). These results indicate that the rewiring null provides valid statistical inference when the unidirectional bridge edge configuration is enforced.

The member-level stratified null provides complementary evidence that does not depend on bridge edge fraction, because it operates on the fixed propagated signal rather than the propagation operator. In KF-CHD, member-level null_z = 1.39 (p = 0.057), indicating signal concentrated primarily at the pathway node rather than distributed across member genes, consistent with a subset of cilia variant genes driving mass to the ciliopathy cluster through the mechanistic network. In KF-NBL, member_mean null_z = 2.43 (p = 0.003), suggesting more distributed signal across pathway member genes, consistent with the broader neural crest character of the NBL seed neighborhood. The two nulls are therefore not redundant: the pathway-node rewiring null tests direct signal accumulation at the pathway concept node; the member-level null tests whether that pathway’s constituent genes carry disproportionate propagated signal. Their convergence in KF-NBL and partial convergence in KF-CHD together support that the ciliopathy signal is not explained by graph topology alone.

Limitations and scope

The benchmark graph is a controlled 1-hop projection of the DDKG using a specific SAB selection. Results are specific to this graph slice and should not be generalized to the full DDKG without validation on expanded exports. The finding that pathway nodes are structurally inaccessible under mechanistic-only propagation applies to this graph configuration; it may change with additional SAB selections that introduce direct mechanistic gene-pathway edges from other source vocabularies.

The SAB selection used here was designed to isolate ontology-aligned mechanistic and pathway representations from association-derived evidence layers, enabling clean attribution in the ablation design. It was not a comprehensive survey of available DDKG content. The contribution of individual source vocabularies to pathway scoring outcomes (which SABs drive which signals) has not been fully characterized and is identified as a priority for future analysis (Supplementary Table ST6 provides the predicate-level breakdown as a starting point). Results may differ on DDKG versions with different SAB coverage, and characterizing BIFO conditioning behavior across graph substrates is a natural direction for follow-on work.

Gene-level recovery metrics are near ceiling in the curated benchmark due to the small held-out set and strongly connected seed neighborhood. These metrics become more discriminating at cohort scale with variant-derived seeds. BIFO as implemented here is best understood as a biologically constrained propagation framework with a reference DDKG implementation rather than a formal ontology in the description-logic sense; the flow class vocabulary and admissibility rules constitute an operational specification that can be extended or reconfigured for other graph representations.

The 15-gene CHD benchmark pool is curated from prior knowledge, which bounds generalization claims beyond this illustrative benchmark. The exhaustive 3,003-split resampling addresses seed-composition sensitivity within this pool but does not substitute for an independent benchmark on a different disease or knowledge graph. The Kids First cohort results provide independent biological validation in the large gene set regime but rely on the same underlying graph and BIFO configuration. Validation on additional disease cohorts, knowledge graph versions, and graph substrates remains important future work.

Conclusion

BIFO provides a principled approach to a problem that affects any inference task on heterogeneous biological graphs: the inability to distinguish biologically meaningful signal from incidental connectivity when mechanistic, associative, and annotation edges are treated equivalently. By making admissibility explicit at the level of the propagation operator, BIFO converts graph complexity from a liability into a structured substrate for biological inference. The two-layer architecture finding establishes that ontology-aligned pathway annotations occupy a structurally distinct layer from mechanistic gene relationships, and that explicit bridge admission is necessary for pathway-level inference regardless of how rich the mechanistic layer is. The KF cohort results show that this framework recovers biologically coherent signal at a scale and in a regime where standard methods lose discriminative power. The convergence of graph-based propagation and direct overlap enrichment on the same ciliopathy signal, across two diseases with known shared developmental vulnerability, supports that the recovered biology is real. Extending BIFO to additional graph substrates, source vocabularies, and inference tasks is the natural direction for this work.

SM1: Variant Processing and Gene Aggregation

Kids First harmonized sequencing and alignment. Whole-genome sequencing (WGS) data were obtained from two independent germline cohorts: NIH dbGAP phs001138 (congenital heart defects, n=1121 total probands; n=697 with complete WGS data used in this analysis) and NIH dbGAP phs001436 (neuroblastoma, n=554 total probands; n=460 used in this analysis). All samples were sequenced on Illumina platforms using paired-end protocols. Raw sequencing reads were processed by the Kids First Data Resource Center pipelines, aligned to the human reference genome build GRCh38 using BWA (Burrows-Wheeler alignment) algorithms, and variant discovery was performed with the Genome Analysis Toolkit (GATK) Best Practices workflow. Cohorts were harmonized to ensure consistent reference build, alignment parameters, and variant-calling procedures. For cohorts originally processed using joint genotyping in family-based designs (e.g., trios or duos), multi-sample VCFs were decomposed into proband-sample VCFs for downstream per-individual filtering and aggregation. Cohorts sequenced as proband-only were retained as individual-level VCFs. All variants analyzed represent germline calls.

Variant quality filtering. Multi-allelic sites were split into biallelic records to ensure consistent downstream filtering and annotation. Variant-level and genotype-level quality control was performed using bcftools (v1.30) to ensure consistency across all cohorts. Only variants annotated with FILTER=PASS, as determined by the GATK variant quality score recalibration (VQSR) or hard-filtering framework, were retained. At the genotype level, we required a minimum genotype quality (GQ) of 20, corresponding to an estimated posterior genotype accuracy of ≥99%, and a minimum read depth (DP) of 10 reads to ensure adequate sequencing support at each locus. An initial broad population frequency filter of MAF ≤1% in gnomAD was applied at this stage to remove common variants unlikely to be disease-relevant, consistent with standard rare disease variant processing pipelines. Genotype-level filtering was applied prior to aggregation across individuals to ensure that only high-confidence variant calls contributed to downstream analyses.

Pathogenicity classification. To enrich for variants with predicted functional impact, we implemented a complementary pathogenicity prioritization strategy integrating both consequence-based and model-based annotations. First, variants were functionally annotated using the Ensembl Variant Effect Predictor (VEP) [23], incorporating transcript consequence, gene assignment, and predicted functional impact categories. Variants annotated by Ensembl VEP as having HIGH impact (including predicted loss-of-function consequences such as stop-gain, frameshift, and canonical splice-site variants) were retained. Second, variants were evaluated using AutoGVP [19], a supervised pathogenicity prediction framework that integrates functional, evolutionary, and annotation-based features. Variants classified as “Pathogenic” or “Likely_Pathogenic” by AutoGVP were retained. Variants meeting either criterion (VEP HIGH impact or AutoGVP “Pathogenic/Likely pathogenic” classification) were included in the final analysis set. This approach enabled capture of canonical loss-of-function alleles as well as predicted deleterious missense or regulatory variants with strong pathogenic support. Both annotation steps were performed against the GRCh38 reference and corresponding Ensembl gene models. The final analytical dataset comprised high-confidence germline variants that passed GATK variant-level filters, met stringent genotype-level depth and quality thresholds, and were predicted to be deleterious by VEP and/or AutoGVP. This harmonized filtering framework ensured consistent variant quality across both cohorts and enriched the dataset for variants with a high prior probability of functional relevance. All downstream gene-level aggregation, cross-cohort comparisons, and enrichment analyses were performed using this curated set of high-confidence pathogenic variants.

Population allele frequency filtering. Variants were filtered against population allele frequency estimates from the Genome Aggregation Database (gnomAD) v3.1 [20], which provides allele frequencies across diverse global populations aligned to GRCh38. For this study, we applied a more stringent analytical threshold of MAF ≤0.001 (0.1%) using the gnomAD v3.1 non-cancer popmax allele frequency (the maximum allele frequency observed across any gnomAD ancestry group, excluding cancer cohorts). This threshold is tenfold more stringent than the initial processing filter, restricts analyses to ultra-rare variants consistent with Mendelian or strong-effect rare disease genetics, and matches the filtering parameters used in the companion U24 cross-cohort enrichment analysis (Stear et al., CFDE Meeting 2026). Variants exceeding this frequency threshold in any gnomAD ancestry group were excluded regardless of their pathogenicity classification.

Gene aggregation. For each cohort, genes harboring at least one qualifying P/LP variant in any proband were aggregated into a per-cohort gene list. Carrier count-filtered subsets requiring ≥2 or ≥3 independent probands carrying qualifying variants in the same gene were generated to assess signal stability under increasingly stringent filters.

Background burden gene exclusion. Twenty genes representing common carrier burden from well-characterized recessive Mendelian conditions were excluded from both cohort gene lists prior to pathway analysis. These genes are present at elevated carrier frequency in any unselected pediatric sequencing cohort at MAF ≤0.001 regardless of disease phenotype, and their inclusion would inflate background signal without contributing disease-specific biological information. The complete exclusion list is: ABCA4, BCHE, CD36, COL4A5, FLG, G6PD, GJB2, KRT71, KRT86, MYO15A, MYO1A, MYO3A, MYO7A, OBSCN, PADI3, PAH, TBL1Y, TCHH, TTN, USH2A.

UMLS CUI resolution. Gene symbols were mapped to UMLS Concept Unique Identifiers (CUIs) for use as pipeline seed inputs using seed_cui_lookup.py, which queries the DDKG for HGNC-SAB concept nodes matching each gene symbol. Of 1,287 CHD seed genes, 1,276 (99.1%) resolved to UMLS CUIs; of 1,406 NBL seed genes, 1,395 (99.2%) resolved. Genes failing CUI resolution were excluded from graph analysis. Final seed counts used in all reported analyses are 1,276 (KF-CHD) and 1,395 (KF-NBL).

Output. Seed gene files for both cohorts at all carrier-count filter levels are provided in the repository at data/cohorts/chd/ and data/cohorts/nbl/.

SM2: Neo4j Export Queries

Graph neighborhoods for all benchmarks and cohort analyses were exported from the Data Distillery Knowledge Graph (DDKG) using Neo4j cypher-shell. Each export follows a four-query pattern: (1) a verification query checking gene symbol-to-CUI resolution; (2) a node export collecting all concept nodes in the 1-hop neighborhood with SAB-priority resolution; (3) an edge export collecting seed↔︎neighbor edges; and (4) a targeted pathway membership edge export collecting gene-to-pathway edges within the neighborhood. Exported CSV files are cleaned using clean_cypher_output.py to remove cypher-shell formatting artifacts before pipeline ingestion.

All query files are provided in the repository at cypher/. The four-query structure is shared across all exports; cohort-specific files differ only in the seed CUI list and output file names.

Node SAB priority ordering. All node export queries resolve each concept to a single source vocabulary (SAB) using a CASE-ordered preference: HGNC (0) > NCBIGENE (1) > MSIGDB (2) > REACTOME (3) > GO (4) > MONDO (5) > OMIM (6) > all others (99). This ordering ensures that gene concepts resolve to their HGNC identifier when available, which is required for entity class assignment in bifo_conditioning.py.

Edge filtering. All edge export queries exclude CODE, PREF_TERM, STY, and ISA relationship types, which carry graph infrastructure rather than biological content. These are the same relationship types excluded during BIFO conditioning; excluding them at export reduces file size without affecting downstream results.

Pathway membership edges. Pathway membership edges connect two hop-1 nodes (a pathway concept and a gene concept) and are therefore absent from the seed↔︎hop1 edge export. They are collected in a separate targeted query using six membership predicates: pathway_associated_with_gene, inverse_pathway_associated_with_gene, has_signature_gene, inverse_has_signature_gene, process_involves_gene, and gene_plays_role_in_process. These predicates correspond to the Pathway Contribution flow class in BIFO and are the structural bridge edges between the mechanistic gene layer and the pathway annotation layer.

Post-export merge. After cleaning, edge files are merged using pandas concat (not shell cat) to preserve CSV headers and avoid duplicate header rows. For KF cohort runs the merge combines the raw edge file and the pathway membership edge file into a single edges_all.csv input for bifo_conditioning.py. Merge commands are documented in comments at the end of each cypher file.

The B1 and B2 baselines are one-tailed hypergeometric tests applied per pathway. Both baselines run in one of two regimes selected by the --small-universe command-line flag. The appropriate regime is selected based on query size relative to the gene universe, and is reported alongside results in Methods §§7, 9, 13, 14, 15.

The standard regime is appropriate when the seed query is small relative to the universe, in which case the survival function does not underflow and rank ordering is preserved naturally. It is not appropriate for the full KF cohort analyses, where 1,276–1,395 seeds against ~22,600 background genes drives the survival function below 10^-300 for every pathway with any meaningful overlap, returning p = 0.0 from hypergeom.sf and eliminating rank discrimination entirely.

The log-space regime addresses this by computing hypergeom.logsf directly and ranking pathways by log-p-value rather than by p-value. The displayed p-value is reconstructed via np.exp(log_p) only when log_p > -700; below that threshold the displayed p is reported as 0.0 to avoid np.exp underflow, but the underlying log-p-value is preserved internally for ranking. This means rank ordering is preserved down to whatever precision logsf provides, which extends well below the float64 floor that defeats sf. Benjamini–Hochberg correction is applied across the log-p-values by sorting on log-p and adjusting; the correction is mathematically valid in either regime since BH is monotone in p-value.

The change of universe between regimes is structural rather than cosmetic. In the standard regime, restricting the universe to ~4,000 pathway-member genes would change the test’s null hypothesis (it would test whether seeds are over-represented among one specific subset of the gene universe, rather than against the genome-equivalent background). In the log-space regime, the smaller universe is appropriate because the question being asked is whether the seed gene list is enriched for pathway membership conditional on being a pathway-annotated gene, not whether it is enriched relative to the full genome. The two questions are biologically different but each is sensible for its setting.

SM3.2 Preranked GSEA (B3 / B3b)

The B3 and B3b baselines compute the preranked GSEA enrichment score of Subramanian et al. [15]. The implementation is a from-scratch numpy-based computation in pipeline/baseline_enrichment.py (function preranked_gsea_enrichment) rather than a third-party package such as fgsea or gseapy. The reason is that standard packages return permutation-based p-values, and our evaluation framework requires rank-based metrics (Average Precision, Precision@k against a held-out reference pathway set) directly comparable to the BIFO-PPR pathway ranking, which does not produce p-values at all.

For each pathway, the algorithm computes the maximum signed deviation of a running-sum statistic over genes ranked by descending PPR score (raw graph for B3; BIFO-conditioned graph for B3b):

  • Hit weighting uses absolute PPR score for member genes, the p = 1 weighted variant in the original GSEA formulation. When all hit genes have zero PPR score, the implementation falls back to unweighted hit counts (a defensive case that is rarely triggered with PPR-propagated seeds).
  • Miss penalty is uniform across non-member genes: 1 / n_misses, where n_misses is the count of non-member genes in the gene universe.
  • The maximum-deviation enrichment score (which can be positive for enrichment or negative for depletion) is the standard GSEA enrichment score; for this paper only positive enrichment is reported.
  • The leading-edge gene fraction is reported alongside the enrichment score.

The pathway membership map is identical to the one used by BIFO-PPR: drawn from the same MSigDB and WikiPathways pathway concept nodes, with the same 8–300 member size filter applied. For the KF cohorts this gives n = 2,130 pathways evaluated. The gene set universe is matched 1:1 across BIFO-PPR and the GSEA arms.

Pathways are ranked by enrichment score for evaluation against the reference pathway set. No permutation-based null distribution is computed for the GSEA arms. The standard GSEA permutation null is designed for hypothesis testing on transcriptomic ranking and provides a normalized enrichment score (NES) and FDR-adjusted p-value; neither is needed for our rank-based comparison metrics. Computing one would require defining a permutation procedure for PPR scores (which themselves arise from a deterministic graph computation given fixed seeds, with no obvious null perturbation); we did not pursue this. The omission is a design choice tied to the rank-based evaluation framework, not a failure to compute a needed statistic.

SM3.3 Resampling Fisher precision

The resampling Fisher baselines in pipeline/chd_resampling_exhaustive.py (benchmark exhaustive resampling) and pipeline/kf_resampling.py (KF cohort bootstrap resampling) use standard-precision hypergeom.sf exclusively. These scripts do not implement a log-space regime. This is appropriate because the query sizes in the resampling settings (10 to 30 seeds per draw) do not trigger the p-value floor collapse that necessitates the log-space regime in the full-cohort analyses; standard-precision computation preserves rank ordering throughout.

Either way, the lay reading “ciliopathy and Hedgehog are co-enriched” is too simple. The cohort is enriched for ciliary-structure lesions, not for Hedgehog-transduction lesions, and the analysis correctly distinguishes the two.

Some of these are bona fide CHD-relevant: SCN5A and CACNA1C variants disrupt cardiac conduction and are linked to Long-QT/Brugada and to structural anomalies; PKD1L1 is essential for left-right asymmetry signaling at the ciliary node (Field et al. Development 2011, PMID: 21307095, and Vetrini et al. confirmed PKD1L1 in human heterotaxy). However, “Ionophore activity” being the top well-calibrated pathway warrants skepticism. The seed-member-score profile is extreme: PKD1L1 = 1.00, CUBN = 0.999, CACNA1S = 0.999, SLCO1B1 = 0.999, ATP7B = 0.999, then everything else ≤ 0.0002. This is theme redundancy: one biological signal — a small set of high-PPR transporter seeds with extensive shared neighbours in the graph — is being counted four or five times. Consolidate them into a single “ion / solute transporter” finding when describing the result. The biology is real (calcium-channel and SLC dysfunction are CHD-relevant) but it is not five independent enrichments.

Notch / neural crest

WP_NEURAL_CREST_DIFFERENTIATION (rank 71, n=98, null_z = 49.5, q = 0.008) and REACTOME_SIGNALING_BY_NOTCH (rank 165, n=174, null_z = 28.3) form a separate, biologically distinct cluster. NOTCH2 is the dominant seed driver (score 1.00 in the neural crest pathway), with DMBX1 second. NOTCH2 is the second locus for Alagille syndrome (after JAG1), causing peripheral pulmonary artery stenosis, tetralogy of Fallot, and septal defects; ~94% of Alagille patients have cardiovascular involvement and the most common lesion is branch pulmonary artery stenosis (Turnpenny & Ellard, Eur J Hum Genet 2012, PMID: 21847141). NOTCH2 also drives Hajdu-Cheney syndrome via gain-of-function variants in the PEST domain. CTNNB1 (β-catenin) and FGFR2 and DLX5 in the same pathway tie the signal to neural-crest-derived structures of the cardiac outflow tract — the conotruncal CHD compartment (TOF, persistent truncus, DORV) is largely a neural-crest disease. The Notch pathway hit therefore likely reflects a different anatomical subset of probands than the ciliopathy hit reflects. Follow-up: a within-cohort stratification of NOTCH2-carrier probands vs. CEP290/CC2D2A-carrier probands by anatomical subtype would test whether the dataset really has two distinct mechanistic subgroups rather than one mixed population.

DNA damage response and BRCA-related biology

WP_DNA_IRDAMAGE_AND_CELLULAR_RESPONSE_VIA_ATR (rank 58), WP_DNA_DAMAGE_RESPONSE (rank 135), BIOCARTA_ATRBRCA_PATHWAY (rank 115), WP_INTEGRATED_CANCER_PATHWAY (rank 91), and HALLMARK_G2M_CHECKPOINT (rank 127, NaN null_z) form a third theme, driven by BRCA2, FANCA, BRCA1, RAD50, ATM, FANCD2, MRE11, CHEK2. This is a less expected enrichment for a structural CHD cohort and warrants careful framing.

Two reasonable interpretations: (a) Pediatric panel artefact — diagnostic exome panels and rare-variant filtering pipelines that target CHD inherently capture high-population-variation tumour-suppressor genes; BRCA2 is one of the most-variable genes in healthy population databases, and rare missense variants are common as incidental findings. (b) Genuine biology — DNA-damage response is required for cardiomyocyte proliferation during fetal heart growth, and primary-cilium-disrupted cells have well-documented genome-instability phenotypes (cilia regulate the DDR via CEP290 and other transition-zone proteins; see Choksi et al., Development 2014 and downstream literature). Cilia and DDR are genuinely linked, so this isn’t necessarily noise — but the BRCA2-dominated single-gene signal deserves caution. Many of these pathways are flagged below as single-gene-dominated.

4. The most distinctive BIFO-specific finding: non-seed driver genes

This is the analytical task that BIFO-PPR was designed for, and the result is striking. Sweeping through every pathway’s influential_nodes_global column and removing genes that ever appear in any pathway’s seed_members, only two genes consistently exceed the global threshold: E2F1 (71 pathways) and CD36 (27 pathways). At the local-but-not-global level, AKT1 (175), NFKB1 (122), PIK3CA (103), GSK3B (82) appear, but those are universal signaling hubs that show up in any large network and cannot be considered a cohort-specific finding. E2F1 and CD36 are genuinely cohort-specific. Neither is in the seed list, so neither would have been flagged by a Fisher overlap analysis.

E2F1 — convergent driver of cell-cycle/DDR pathways

E2F1 emerges as the global influential node in 71 pathways, and the unifying theme is unambiguous: cell-cycle regulation, G1/S transition, RB-pathway control, DNA replication initiation, DDR, apoptosis. Specific pathways include BIOCARTA_SKP2E2F_PATHWAY, REACTOME_CDC6_ASSOCIATION_WITH_THE_ORC_ORIGIN_COMPLEX, REACTOME_INHIBITION_OF_REPLICATION_INITIATION_OF_DAMAGED_DNA_BY_RB1_E2F1, BIOCARTA_P53_PATHWAY, REACTOME_E2F_MEDIATED_REGULATION_OF_DNA_REPLICATION, REACTOME_TP53_REGULATES_TRANSCRIPTION_OF_CELL_CYCLE_GENES, HALLMARK_G2M_CHECKPOINT, REACTOME_INTRINSIC_PATHWAY_FOR_APOPTOSIS, WP_DNA_DAMAGE_RESPONSE, BIOCARTA_G1_PATHWAY. Convergence across 71 distinct pathways on a single biological theme — cell-cycle control downstream of RB — is exactly the kind of signal the BIFO framework is designed to surface.

Why is this biologically plausible for CHD? Cardiomyocyte proliferation is the engine of fetal heart growth; cardiomyocytes proliferate intensely during embryonic chamber formation, then withdraw from the cell cycle shortly after birth (Cardoso et al. Am J Physiol Heart Circ Physiol 2020 / PMC4514102). E2F1 is a central regulator of cardiomyocyte cell-cycle re-entry: overexpression in cardiomyocytes drives DNA synthesis and S-phase entry but with concomitant apoptosis (Vara et al. J Biol Chem 2003, PMID: 12682052; Ebelt et al. Circ Res 2005, PMID: 15718502 — though Ebelt found E2F1 cannot induce DNA synthesis without apoptosis, while E2F2 can). E2F1 protein levels are high in fetal cardiomyocytes and drop to undetectable in adult cardiomyocytes (Vara et al. 2003). So the pathway in which seed genes carry rare variants (BRCA2, ATM, RB1, CDKN2A, CHEK2, CDC45, MRE11, ORC1/6) propagates through the graph to converge on E2F1, which is mechanistically the right node for cardiomyocyte cell-cycle control. The fact that E2F1 itself is not mutated but is the convergent network hub is a textbook BIFO finding.

Follow-up suggestions for the user: (1) Re-examine the seed list for E2F1 directly — ensure it isn’t missing because of variant-filter cutoffs. (2) If trio-level data is available, check whether E2F1 carries any pathogenic variants in the broader (unfiltered) variant set. (3) Consider testing E2F1-target burden as a custom gene set in a Fisher-type test, since BIFO suggests E2F1’s downstream regulon is collectively enriched. (4) Mechanistically, fetal heart looping and septation depend on tightly regulated cardiomyocyte proliferation; perturbations of the RB/E2F axis would predict thin-myocardium / non-compaction phenotypes — worth checking the cohort for those subtypes.

CD36 — convergent driver of metabolic and immune pathways

CD36 emerges as the global influential node in 27 pathways. The theme is also coherent but distinct: lipid uptake, fatty acid metabolism, PPAR signalling, scavenger receptor function, innate immune / TLR signalling, platelet activation, adipogenesis. Specific pathways include REACTOME_FREE_FATTY_ACIDS_REGULATE_INSULIN_SECRETION, WP_PPAR_SIGNALING_PATHWAY, HALLMARK_FATTY_ACID_METABOLISM, REACTOME_BINDING_AND_UPTAKE_OF_LIGANDS_BY_SCAVENGER_RECEPTORS, REACTOME_REGULATION_OF_LIPID_METABOLISM_BY_PPARALPHA, REACTOME_TOLL_LIKE_RECEPTOR_TLR1_TLR2_CASCADE, REACTOME_PLATELET_ACTIVATION_SIGNALING_AND_AGGREGATION, HALLMARK_IL6_JAK_STAT3_SIGNALING, REACTOME_INTERLEUKIN_4_AND_INTERLEUKIN_13_SIGNALING.

CD36 (also SR-B2 / FAT, fatty acid translocase) is the principal long-chain fatty acid transporter at the cardiomyocyte sarcolemma and accounts for ~50–70% of cardiac FA uptake (Pepino et al., Annu Rev Nutr 2014; Glatz et al. Physiol Rev 2024). Cardiac CD36 deletion in mice protects against high-fat-diet cardiomyopathy in some models but accelerates pressure-overload heart failure in others, depending on the substrate context (Sung et al. Sci Rep 2017, PMID: 29097701). Critically for this cohort: cardiac substrate preference undergoes a major perinatal switch, from glucose-dominant in fetal cardiomyocytes to FA-dominant after birth, and CD36 is induced precisely at this transition by PPARα and long-chain fatty acid signaling (van der Lee et al. J Lipid Res 2000; Lopaschuk & Jaswal J Cardiovasc Pharmacol 2010). Its convergent appearance here is therefore plausibly tied to perinatal cardiac metabolic maturation — a process that is dysregulated in CHD where neonates often present with metabolic stress.

Caveats specific to CD36: (a) The CD36 null allele is unusually frequent in some populations (up to ~26% in some African and Asian populations per Glatz et al.), so without ancestry breakdown one cannot be sure whether the CD36 signal reflects cohort-specific rare-variant connectivity or knowledge-graph hub bias. (b) Many of the CD36-implicated pathways (TLR cascades, IL-6/JAK/STAT, antigen presentation) are immune pathways where CD36 is a bona fide scavenger receptor but the connection to a structural CHD cohort is indirect. (c) The seed_member columns for many CD36-implicated pathways contain few or no high-scoring seeds (e.g., REACTOME_TOLL_LIKE_RECEPTOR_TLR1_TLR2_CASCADE has just MAPK7, MAP2K1 with score 0.0000), so the pathway score is being carried almost entirely by graph propagation, not by direct seed overlap. This is the BIFO use case at its purest, but it also means the signal needs experimental validation rather than face-value acceptance.

Follow-up suggestions: (1) Test whether CD36 carries rare variants in the cohort below the original CADD≥20 threshold. (2) Check whether the seed list contains genes whose products interact with CD36 (LPL, FABP3, FABP4, SLC27A6, PPARα/PPARG) — these may be the bridge nodes. (3) If neonatal metabolic data is available for any probands (lactate, fatty acid panels, ketones), test whether CD36-pathway burden correlates with metabolic-decompensation episodes after surgical palliation.

5. Skeptical pass — pathways that look strong but probably shouldn’t drive interpretation

Several patterns recur in the well-calibrated top 200 that the user should treat with caution:

Single-gene-dominated pathways. When seed_member_scores shows one gene at 1.0 and all other members near 0.0, the pathway is being lit up by that one gene’s PPR, not by distributed cohort biology. The pathway name will then mislead. Examples: Synaptic Transmission (rank 56) is essentially DRD4 alone; Glycolysis (rank 116) is GPI alone; REACTOME_BILE_ACID_AND_BILE_SALT_METABOLISM (rank 101) is SLCO1B1 alone; WP_GPCRS_OTHER (rank 143) is DRD4 alone; WP_RAS_AND_BRADYKININ_PATHWAYS_IN_COVID19 and WP_SARSCOV2_MITOCHONDRIAL_INTERACTIONS are ACE alone (a COVID-19 ACE2 connection that is biologically irrelevant to CHD); WP_PHOSPHODIESTERASES_IN_NEURONAL_FUNCTION is ADCY10 alone; WP_INTEGRATED_BREAST_CANCER_PATHWAY and WP_HOMOLOGOUS_RECOMBINATION are BRCA2 alone. In every one of these, the appropriate biological summary is “a single gene with rare variants in the cohort happens to be a member of this pathway” — which is much weaker than the pathway-level rank suggests.

Theme redundancy. I called this out for the SLC/transporter cluster above. The cilium cluster has the same property — Joubert syndrome, ciliopathies, cilium assembly, basal-body anchoring, ciliary-CRISPR-screen genes are all one finding. Likewise the DNA-damage cluster (BRCA-ATR, ATR/ATM responses, G1/S, G2/M checkpoint, integrated cancer pathway) is one finding driven by the BRCA2/ATM/RAD50/CHEK2/MRE11 sub-block of the seed list. Counting these as separate hits inflates the apparent breadth of biology in the cohort.

Very large pathways with high rank but modest null_z. Rank 75 (Apoptosis, n=238, null_z = 44.8) and rank 79 (REACTOME_SLC_MEDIATED_TRANSMEMBRANE_TRANSPORT, n=246, null_z = 43.5) are large enough that some of their rank reflects connectivity rather than specific signal. Rank 167 (REACTOME_METABOLISM_OF_CARBOHYDRATES, n=287, null_z = 19.6) is borderline. Compare these to Rank 9 (Ionophore activity, n=189, null_z = 85.5) — same size class but a much higher null_z, so the calibration test correctly distinguishes specific from generic signal.

High rank but NaN null_z (degenerate). HALLMARK gene sets are deliberately broad and tend to be hub-like in any biomedical knowledge graph. They are not biologically meaningless, but they cannot be assigned a specificity p-value, so they should be cited as exploratory only.

Skewed neighbor_z_global distributions reveal hub-driven scoring. Across nearly every well-calibrated top pathway, neighbor_z_global has the same shape: max ≈ 23.7, p75 ≈ p50 ≈ p25 ≈ −0.04. That means each of these pathways has a small number (often 1–3) of globally extraordinary neighbours, and a flat distribution of unremarkable ones. The “max ≈ 23.7” recurs because the same handful of genes (CEP290, PKD1L1, BRCA2, NOTCH2, AAAS, SLCO1B1, etc., plus the network hub E2F1) sit on the boundary of many pathways. This is consistent with what BIFO is designed to do — propagate signal from a few high-PPR seeds — but it does mean that a substantial portion of the apparent diversity of significant pathways is being generated by a small number of highly-connected genes appearing repeatedly.

6. Recommendations to the analyst

In rough order of priority:

  1. Stratify the cohort by anatomical CHD subtype. The mixture of ciliopathy + Notch-neural-crest signal almost certainly reflects two anatomically distinct subgroups (heterotaxy/AVSD vs. conotruncal/PPS) — splitting the analysis would sharpen both stories.
  2. Investigate E2F1 and CD36 directly. These are the cohort-specific BIFO findings. Check seed-list inclusion thresholds, look at burden in their immediate interactome, and consider experimental validation (cardiomyocyte proliferation assays for E2F1; perinatal metabolic phenotyping for CD36).
  3. Consolidate redundant themes when reporting. Five “transporter-disorder” pathways and four “DDR/BRCA” pathways are each one finding.
  4. Resolve the Hedgehog negative-enrichment surprise. Check whether canonical Hh transducers (PTCH1, SMO, SUFU, GLI2, KIF7) receive PPR mass, and whether the result generalises across other CHD cohorts processed the same way.
  5. Complete the FAIR provenance. Paste the Cypher export query, fill in real analyst attribution, and add a CHD-subtype demographics table before submission.

The bottom line: this is a clean, recognisably-CHD result whose dominant mechanistic axis is rare-variant burden in primary-cilium / transition-zone genes plus a NOTCH2-anchored neural-crest signal — exactly the architecture predicted by Watkins et al. for the recessive/rare-inherited compartment of CHD genetics. The two distinctive non-seed network drivers, E2F1 and CD36, point at cardiomyocyte proliferation and perinatal cardiac metabolism as plausible secondary mechanisms that would not have been detected by Fisher-style enrichment on the seed list alone.

A critical methodological flag before interpretation

Before the biology, one finding that affects how to read every result below: I parsed all 2,196 rows of the TSV and compared seed_members to influential_nodes_local and influential_nodes_global for each pathway. In every single pathway, influential_nodes_global is identical to seed_members, and influential_nodes_local is always a subset of seeds (often empty). There are zero non-seed genes recovered as influential drivers anywhere in this run. This means Task 1 (non-obvious BIFO drivers) and Task 2 (convergent process analysis for those drivers) cannot be performed on this output — the very signal the analytical instructions ask me to mine is absent from the data.

This is the single most important observation about the run. There are a few possible explanations, and the analyst should determine which applies before publishing:

  1. The graph propagation may not have been run with the broader gene-node universe enabled (the influential-nodes calculation may have been limited only to seeds rather than to all HGNC nodes neighboring each pathway).
  2. The 1.96-σ global threshold may be working as intended but the seed set is so large (1,395 genes) that virtually any HGNC neighbor of a pathway scoring above the global threshold is also a seed by construction — saturating the seed list dilutes BIFO’s main differentiating advantage.
  3. There may be a bug in the influential-nodes export that emits seeds rather than the full neighbor set.

The neighbor_z_global summaries are consistent with explanation (2): the global max is essentially identical (~4.56–4.58) across nearly every pathway, suggesting one or two graph-level hub genes (TP53 / JAK1 / MAPK1 from the run-level summary) saturate the global tail for nearly every pathway. With ~1,395 seeds out of ~20,000 HGNC genes (~7% of the genome), the seed set is large enough that most pathway-relevant genes are already inside it, and BIFO is functioning more like a sophisticated overlap-weighted ranker than a network-discovery tool. This is itself an important meta-finding: with a seed list this large, BIFO’s distinctive ability to surface non-obvious drivers is mostly nullified, and the results should be read as a topology-aware enrichment of the input list, not as a discovery of new genes outside it.

I will now interpret the biology of the top pathways and seed-level drivers, but flag throughout where the lack of non-obvious drivers limits interpretation.

The dominant biological themes in the top 50

The top 50 pathways group into a small number of biological themes that recur in slightly different forms — true pathway redundancy that should be consolidated rather than counted as independent hits.

Theme 1: DNA damage response and homologous recombination — the central, biologically-coherent signal

This is the most compelling and disease-appropriate finding. DNA Repair (rank 5, null_z = 14.7, q = 0.012) is anchored by TP53, ATM, the FANC core complex (FANCA/D2/E/G/M, FAN1), the MMR machinery (MSH2/3/6, MLH1, PMS2, EXO1), the NER machinery (ERCC2/3/4/5/6/8, UVSSA), and the homologous recombination apparatus (BRCA2, BARD1, BRIP1, RAD50, RAD51C/D, RAD54B/L, MRE11, ATR, TP53BP1). Closely related pathways recur: Mismatch Repair (rank 64, null_z = 8.9), PID_FANCONI_PATHWAY (rank 119, null_z = 5.1), REACTOME_HDR_THROUGH_HOMOLOGOUS_RECOMBINATION (rank 149, null_z = 3.7), REACTOME_HOMOLOGOUS_DNA_PAIRING_AND_STRAND_EXCHANGE (rank 156, null_z = 4.5), BIOCARTA_ATRBRCA_PATHWAY (rank 111, null_z = 5.6), and WP_DNA_REPAIR_PATHWAYS_FULL_NETWORK (rank 42, null_z = 6.6). REACTOME_DNA_REPAIR (rank 49) has a much weaker null_z of 2.87 — it’s a 280-gene set whose ranking is partly buoyed by size, and it should be down-weighted relative to the smaller, more specific Mismatch Repair (n=11) and Fanconi (n=46) hits.

This theme is exactly what a careful reader of the neuroblastoma germline literature would predict and is the strongest biological signal in the dataset. BARD1 has been the single most replicable rare-variant gene in pediatric NBL — Randall et al. showed in JNCI 2024 that germline BARD1 loss-of-function variants induce haploinsufficiency, defective RAD51 focus formation at double-strand breaks, and increased sensitivity to PARP inhibition in isogenic NBL cell models (Randall MP et al., J Natl Cancer Inst 2024;116(1):138–148, doi:10.1093/jnci/djad182, PMID 36778420). Kim et al. demonstrated that 13.9% of 786 NBL probands carry P/LP variants in cancer predisposition genes, with BARD1 the most enriched (OR ≈ 32, J Natl Cancer Inst 2024;116(1):149, doi:10.1093/jnci/djad183, PMID 37688579). The presence in this cohort’s seed list of BARD1, BRCA2, BRIP1, ATM, CHEK2 (in REACTOME_DNA_REPAIR at rank 49), MUTYH, PALB2-pathway partners, and the FANC complex, together driving multiple HRR/MMR/Fanconi pathways, recapitulates that published predisposition profile and is a strong positive control for the analysis. Follow-up of practical interest: patients harboring biallelic or compound DNA-repair variants are candidates for PARP-inhibitor trials, and Randall et al. specifically demonstrated PARPi sensitivity in BARD1-mutant NBL cells. Ranking probands in this cohort by HRD-related germline variant load and correlating with outcome and treatment response is a directly actionable next step.

Theme 2: Ciliopathy / Hedgehog — the pre-specified reference, with mixed performance

The reference set was ciliopathy and Hedgehog-signaling pathways. WP_CILIOPATHIES (rank 3, null_z = 18.9, q = 0.012) is a strong positive-control hit, with seeds covering the canonical ciliopathy/IFT machinery: CEP290, CEP164, BBS4, BBS9, BBS10, AHI1, NPHP1/3/4, CC2D2A, IQCB1, ALMS1, TMEM67/107/138/237, ARL13B, the IFT-A/B complexes (IFT52/81/140/172), DYNC2H1, DYNC2I2, the EVC/EVC2 cassette, and KIF7. WP_JOUBERT_SYNDROME (rank 17, null_z = 10.5), WP_GENES_RELATED_TO_PRIMARY_CILIUM_DEVELOPMENT_BASED_ON_CRISPR (rank 31, null_z = 8.9, in_reference = TRUE), and REACTOME_CILIUM_ASSEMBLY (rank 32, null_z = 6.1, in_reference = TRUE) reinforce the same theme. However, the picture for Hedgehog signaling itself is striking and worth flagging: while the cilium-assembly pathways score well, REACTOME_SIGNALING_BY_HEDGEHOG (rank 228, null_z = 0.5, q = 0.90), HALLMARK_HEDGEHOG_SIGNALING (rank 722, null_z degenerate), REACTOME_HEDGEHOG_OFF_STATE (rank 347), REACTOME_HEDGEHOG_ON_STATE (rank 482), and PID_HEDGEHOG_GLI_PATHWAY (rank 544) are not significant. The cilium scaffolding side of the reference set is enriched; the Hedgehog signal-transduction side is not.

This is biologically interesting rather than a failure of the analysis. Neuroblasts arise from neural-crest-derived sympathoadrenal precursors that bear primary cilia during migration (Toro-Tapia & Das, Int J Mol Sci 2021;22:3176, PMID 33809731); SHH signaling is required for neural-crest patterning and sympathetic-lineage specification. But several groups have shown that in established neuroblastoma cells, primary cilia are lost and Hedgehog signaling appears to act in a tumor-suppressive rather than tumor-promoting role (Wickström M et al., the GLI-inhibitor work; Kirschmann J et al., Cancers 2021;13:1908, PMID 33920990). The clean enrichment of cilium assembly genes alongside the absence of HH signal-transduction enrichment is consistent with a model in which germline rare variants in this cohort are perturbing the structural/developmental cilium machinery (relevant during neural-crest specification of the sympathetic lineage in utero) rather than the canonical SMO–GLI cascade. CEP290, BBS9, BBS10, AHI1 — all loaded with rare variants here — are the kind of genes that, if biallelically disrupted, cause Joubert/Bardet-Biedl syndromes that include neurodevelopmental phenotypes; in heterozygous form they may contribute to the developmental-context background that allows neuroblast specification to go awry. A specific follow-up question: does the germline ciliary-gene burden track with NBL risk-group, age at diagnosis, or anatomic origin (adrenal vs paraspinal)? The literature on the role of NCC primary cilia in PNS specification (Toro-Tapia & Das 2021) gives a directly testable model.

A skeptical caveat: WP_CILIOPATHIES is a 169-gene composite, and 64 seed members overlap with it. With ~7% of genes in the seed list, having 64 ciliopathy genes is partly an indication that the input gene list itself was enriched for known recessive Mendelian disease genes (the seed list reads like a clinical exome panel — note ABC transporters, collagens, keratins, SLC transporters, COL11A1, FBN1, FANCs, BBSs, NPHPs all together). Some of the ciliopathy enrichment could therefore reflect gene-panel composition rather than cohort-specific enrichment. Comparing this enrichment to a control cohort run through the same pipeline would be informative.

Theme 3: Transmembrane transport / channelopathy — likely partly an artifact of pathway size

Ranks 1, 2, 16, 18, 26, and 51 are essentially the same biology repackaged: Ionophore activity (rank 1), Transmembrane Transport (rank 2), Nucleotide Metabolism (rank 16, dominated by ABC transporter members), REACTOME_DISORDERS_OF_TRANSMEMBRANE_TRANSPORTERS (rank 18), REACTOME_SLC_TRANSPORTER_DISORDERS (rank 26), and REACTOME_ION_CHANNEL_TRANSPORT (rank 51). The seed members are the same recurring cast: SLC family members, ABC transporters, voltage-gated channels (SCN5A, SCN9A, KCNQ1, CACNA1G/H/I, RYR1, RYR2), connexins (GJB4), TRP channels, ATP1A3, ATP7B, CFTR. The neighbor_z_global max in each of these is ~4.57, and the local max is only 1.66–2.06, with a flat distribution (p75 ≈ p50 ≈ p25 ≈ −0.5). This is the textbook signature of a generic-hub enrichment: the signal is broad, distributed, and driven by the sheer number of seed members rather than any single dominant driver.

I would interpret this whole cluster as primarily reflecting (a) the size of the transport/channel gene families in the human genome, (b) the gene-panel composition of the seed set (hundreds of channelopathy/transporter Mendelian-disease genes), and (c) generic graph hub-ness. None of the individual transmembrane-transport pathways have an obvious connection to neuroblastoma biology, with one important exception: voltage-gated and TRP channels are implicated in neural-crest migration and sympathetic-neuron differentiation, and channelopathies can cause autonomic dysfunction. But to pull a neuroblastoma-specific claim out of these pathways would require comparing the cohort-specific transporter variant burden to a control cohort processed through identical pipeline. Without that comparison, this cluster is uninformative — a “filler” theme. My recommendation: do not lead with this in any published interpretation. It is the largest single pathway-redundancy artifact in the top 50.

Theme 4: Metabolism (drug, xenobiotic, amino-acid, carbohydrate, thyroid)

Multiple metabolic pathways score highly: REACTOME_DISEASES_OF_METABOLISM (rank 6, null_z = 13.2, n = 242 — large), WP_AMINO_ACID_METABOLISM (rank 10, null_z = 10.9), Carbohydrate Metabolism (rank 12), Xenobiotic Metabolism (rank 15, top seed CYP1B1), HALLMARK_XENOBIOTIC_METABOLISM (rank 27), Drug Metabolism (rank 41), HALLMARK_ADIPOGENESIS (rank 58, null_z = 36.2 — the highest null_z in the entire run), HALLMARK_OXIDATIVE_PHOSPHORYLATION (rank 75, null_z = 32.6), WP_THYROID_HORMONES_PRODUCTION (rank 25), and REACTOME_THYROXINE_BIOSYNTHESIS (rank 50). The thyroid sub-theme deserves its own note — TSHR is one of the run-level top-five globally influential seed genes, and the top globally influential seeds also include JAK1, MAPK1, JAK2, and TP53. TSHR is unusual to flag in a NBL cohort: it has no established germline NBL predisposition role. Its prominence here is most plausibly explained by it being a strongly connected hub in the Data Distillery KG (a GPCR with many edges to thyroid disease, signaling, GI, and cancer pathways), elevating its global PPR rank as a graph-topology effect rather than a cohort-specific finding. The same caution applies to JAK1/JAK2/MAPK1: these are massive signaling hubs in any KG and appearing as top globally influential seeds is consistent with hub-bias rather than disease-specific signal.

The amino-acid and carbohydrate metabolism enrichment, on the other hand, may be more cohort-meaningful in light of the literature. NBL tumors are metabolically distinctive — MYCN-driven NBL shows altered glutamine and serine metabolism — but more importantly, the seed list contains DDC and TH (tyrosine hydroxylase / DOPA decarboxylase, the catecholamine-synthesis enzymes) which are functional markers of the sympathoadrenal lineage. DDC and TH appear together in the top of WP_AMINO_ACID_METABOLISM (rank 10). Their presence here is consistent with the cohort being selected for a tumor that arises from catecholamine-producing cells; whether this is biologically meaningful or a tautology of cohort selection is worth examining (do these cohort patients have higher catecholamine-pathway variant burden than gnomAD?).

The HALLMARK_ADIPOGENESIS signal (rank 58, null_z = 36.2) is the single highest null_z in the entire run and deserves attention — but a 200-gene Hallmark with broad mitochondrial and metabolic content frequently surfaces in any cohort with any metabolic gene-set burden. Without examining the seed-member spread (which I could not see in the table excerpt for rank 58), I would treat this as suspicious until verified — Hallmark adipogenesis significantly overlaps with oxidative phosphorylation and mitochondrial function, both highly redundant with rank-75 OXPHOS.

Theme 5: Coagulation / complement / extracellular matrix — a surprising and worth-investigating signal

Blood coagulation (rank 7, null_z = 18.7, q = 0.012, just 31 members) is a very tight, specific pathway — only 10 seed members (KLKB1, F11, F7, PROC, F8, ADAMTS13, VWF, SERPINC1, PRKAR1A, VKORC1) but a strong null_z. REACTOME_INTRINSIC_PATHWAY_OF_FIBRIN_CLOT_FORMATION (rank 46) and WP_COMPLEMENT_AND_COAGULATION_CASCADES (rank 45) reinforce. REACTOME_TERMINAL_PATHWAY_OF_COMPLEMENT (rank 38) and Complement Activation (rank 39) add a complement angle, with C9 as the dominant seed gene. REACTOME_EXTRACELLULAR_MATRIX_ORGANIZATION (rank 44) brings in PLG, ADAMTS13, ADAM family, and collagens. This is unexpected in a NBL germline analysis and worth a careful look.

A few biological framings: ADAMTS13 deficiency causes thrombotic thrombocytopenic purpura, and pediatric cancers can have coagulopathies. F8 and VWF are X-linked / autosomal hemostatic genes. Whether this enrichment reflects a real germline coagulation-gene burden in NBL probands or is a cohort-collection artifact (e.g., probands recruited via cancer-genetics clinics may have been more likely to have CBC/coagulation workups that flagged additional germline variants) cannot be told from the analysis alone. Specific follow-up: check whether coagulation-gene variant carriers in this cohort have any clinical phenotype (pre-treatment coagulopathy, thrombosis, surgical-bleeding history), which would convert this from a curiosity into a clinical finding. ADAMTS-family genes (ADAMTS2/3/13/18/19) recurring across DISEASES_OF_METABOLISM and ECM organization suggest an ECM-remodeling sub-signal worth distinguishing from pure hemostasis.

Theme 6: Tissue-development pathways — neural-crest-adjacent biology

Skeletal Development (rank 8, null_z = 19.1) — driven by BMP1, BMP6, EVC, EVC2, PAX1, PPIB, CCN6 — this is a small, focused, high-null_z pathway. EVC/EVC2 also drive WP_CILIOPATHIES. Myogenesis (rank 9, null_z = 14.1) is dominated by RYR1, ACTA1, EMD, NEB, TNNT2, DMPK and is most likely an artifact of having many cardiomyopathy/myopathy genes in a clinical-exome-style seed list rather than a NBL-specific finding. Regulation of Cell Shape (rank 4, null_z = 16.3) is more interesting because it includes DVL1 (Wnt PCP), CEP164 (cilia), MACF1, and the keratin cluster (KRT9/10/12/13/14/16/18/81) — keratins are again likely panel-composition driven, but DVL1 + CEP164 together is suggestive of Wnt-PCP / planar-cell-polarity signaling, which is integral to neural-crest migration and sympathoadrenal specification. Organogenesis (rank 28) and Eye Development (rank 21) and Vision (rank 14) similarly look more like consequences of having retinitis-pigmentosa, Usher-syndrome, and ocular-developmental genes in the seed set than NBL-specific findings.

Theme 7: Nervous-system / synaptic transmission

Synaptic Transmission (rank 29, null_z = 10.4) brings in HTR2C, GRM6, GRIA3, GABBR2, DRD4, CACNA1G, GRIN2B/2D, CNTNAP2, plus TH and TRH — neurotransmitter-receptor and dopamine/thyrotropin-releasing-hormone genes. Combined with the ion-channel signal, this is what one would expect of a sympathoadrenal-lineage cohort: catecholaminergic and synaptic machinery. The most cohort-specific finding here is probably TH (tyrosine hydroxylase) and DDC, which co-occur in WP_AMINO_ACID_METABOLISM and underline the catecholamine-biosynthesis identity of the lineage.

Skeptical pass: pathways to discount or read with caution

Walking through the criteria:

  1. Single-gene-dominated pathways (top seed = 1.0, next < 0.5): I checked this explicitly. None of the top 50 fall into this pattern — the second-highest seed in every case is ≥ 0.99 — so single-gene domination is not a major concern in this run. (This is itself a sign that PPR scores are very tightly compressed across the top-pathway seeds, which is expected when many seeds share many graph edges to the pathway node.)

  2. Very large pathways scoring high. REACTOME_DNA_REPAIR (n=280, rank 49, null_z = 2.87) is borderline — large with modest null_z. REACTOME_DISEASES_OF_METABOLISM (n=242, rank 6, null_z = 13.2) is large but the null_z is high enough to be credible. REACTOME_CILIUM_ASSEMBLY (n=194, rank 32, null_z = 6.1) is the lowest null_z among the cilia hits despite being the most generic — interpret it as a confirmatory composite, not a primary discovery.

  3. High rank but low null_z. REACTOME_DNA_REPAIR (rank 49, null_z = 2.87) and REACTOME_ION_CHANNEL_TRANSPORT (rank 51, null_z = 5.1) are the cleanest examples in the top 50. Their rank reflects pathway size and hub-ness more than specific enrichment, and they should be subordinated to the smaller, more null-specific hits in the same theme.

  4. Pathway redundancy. The transport/channel cluster (ranks 1, 2, 16, 18, 26, 51) collapses into one theme. The cilia cluster (3, 17, 31, 32) collapses into one. The DNA-repair cluster (5, 42, 49, 64, 111, 119, 149, 156, 176) collapses into one. The metabolism cluster (6, 10, 12, 15, 22, 27, 30, 41) collapses into one. In effect, the 50 displayed top pathways resolve to roughly 6–8 distinct biological themes, and a publishable summary should report them at that level of consolidation.

Summary, with what’s left undone

The signal that most clearly maps to known NBL germline biology is the DNA damage repair / homologous recombination axis, anchored by BARD1, BRCA2, ATM, CHEK2, the FANC complex, and MMR genes. This is the strongest, most disease-specific, most literature-supported finding. The cilium-assembly pathways are a clean positive-control hit for the pre-specified reference set, but Hedgehog signal-transduction itself is not enriched — consistent with the published model of NBL as a tumor in which cells lose primary cilia and HH signaling is tumor-suppressive. The transmembrane-transport, channelopathy, myogenesis, and broad metabolism enrichments most likely reflect the panel-style composition of the 1,395-gene seed list and should not be over-interpreted as cohort-specific without comparison to a control cohort run through the same pipeline.

The two analytical tasks I could not complete are tasks 1 and 2 — the BIFO-distinctive non-obvious-driver analysis and convergence analysis — because every influential_nodes_global entry in this run is identical to the corresponding seed_members list. This is the most important methodological observation about the run. It should be diagnosed before the results are published, because a key claimed advantage of BIFO over Fisher-style enrichment (recovery of pathways via graph propagation through non-seed genes) cannot be demonstrated from this output. In a 1,395-gene seed list — large enough to cover ~7% of HGNC — the seeds may simply saturate the global high-PPR tail by construction; if so, the lesson is that BIFO’s discovery advantage will be more visible on smaller, more focused seed lists (e.g. 50–200 genes) than on cohort-scale germline burden lists like this one.

For follow-up: rerunning with a smaller, more curated seed (e.g. only ClinVar-classified P/LP germline variants, perhaps 100–300 genes) would let the influential-nodes-global column do what it was designed for. Stratifying the cohort (high-risk vs low/intermediate, MYCN-amp vs not, age groups) and rerunning per stratum would also let the pathway signal differentiate biologically, since the published evidence (Randall et al. 2024; Kim et al. 2024) shows that the BARD1/HRD signal is concentrated in high-risk cases.
